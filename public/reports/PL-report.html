Executive Summary

Planet Labs operates the world's largest commercial fleet of Earth observation satellites — over 200 satellites imaging the entire land surface of Earth every single day. This gives the company a unique, high-revisit data advantage that no competitor can easily replicate. At $36.55, the stock is near the top of its 52-week range after recovering from a low of $2.79, reflecting growing institutional recognition of the commercial potential of daily satellite imagery at scale. Business Model

Planet Labs generates revenue from three streams: Data and Platform (satellite imagery and analytics sold to customers), Subscription/PRO (higher-frequency tasking and analysis products), and Cloud & Partnerships (embedding Planet data into third-party platforms like AWS, Google Cloud, and Esri). The company's crown jewel is its daily revisit rate — competitors like Maxar and Airbus cannot match the frequency at scale, making Planet's data uniquely suited for change detection, agricultural monitoring, and financial intelligence. The company is still loss-making on a net income basis, though it is approaching operating cash flow breakeven. The key metric to watch is remaining performance obligations (RPO) — a measure of contracted future revenue that gives visibility into the pipeline. Management has been guiding toward sustained non-GAAP profitability in the near term.

Recent Catalysts

AI-driven geospatial analytics demand: The explosion of AI/ML applications has put Planet's data pipeline in focus. Automated crop yield prediction, infrastructure monitoring, and supply chain intelligence all require the kind of frequent, large-area imagery Planet provides. US government contracts: Planet has significant exposure to USGS, NASA, and DoD imagery contracts. Any expansion of defence or intelligence community usage of commercial satellite imagery is a direct catalyst. Path to profitability: Management has committed to adjusted EBITDA breakeven and is guiding toward sustained profitability. Any delay would be a significant negative. Competition from Axiom and others: Maxar (now private) and a cohort of new entrants are building next-generation imaging constellations. Planet's revisit advantage is durable but not permanent.
