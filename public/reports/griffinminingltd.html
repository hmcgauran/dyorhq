Executive Summary

Griffin Mining is a UK-listed precious and base metals producer whose single material asset is the Caijiaying mine in Hebei Province, China. The operation produces gold, zinc, and silver, making it a multi-commodity exposure rather than a pure-play gold miner -- a structural feature that reduces gold price dependence and adds a meaningful zinc by-product credit to the revenue mix. At 278.74 GBX, the stock trades at approximately 8x estimated EPS of 35p, sits roughly 30% below its 52-week high of ~400 GBX, and carries a market capitalisation of approximately GBP150m. The China operating environment is the central risk variable, but the current valuation and the multi-commodity revenue profile make Griffin Mining a credible value case within the UK small-cap precious metals universe.

The BUY thesis rests on three pillars: a sub-10x earnings multiple that does not fully reflect the operational stability of a producing asset, zinc by-product revenue that acts as a natural cost offset and is underappreciated by a market focused on the gold narrative, and a 30% discount to the 52-week high that provides a meaningful margin of safety. China country risk is the primary caveat and warrants ongoing monitoring.

Bottom line: BUY (strong). Caijiaying is a genuine producing asset generating real earnings. The 8x multiple is not demanding for a precious and base metals producer with zinc optionality. Accumulate on any pullback to 230-240 GBX and hold through the 300-350 GBX range as the market re-prices the by-product credit.

Business Model

Griffin Mining's business model is anchored to the Caijiaying mine -- a producing zinc-gold-silver operation located in Hebei Province, approximately 200 kilometres northwest of Beijing. The mine is a long-life, multi-phase operation that has been producing for over a decade, with the orebody hosting significant zinc grades alongside gold and silver. This multi-commodity profile is the defining characteristic of the investment case: unlike a pure-play gold miner, Griffin receives a meaningful revenue contribution from zinc concentrate sales, which provides a degree of insulation from gold price volatility and effectively reduces the apparent all-in cost of gold production.

The Chinese operating environment is governed by the company's Chinese subsidiary structure and the applicable Chinese mining regulations. Griffin operates through a joint venture or wholly-owned subsidiary arrangement with Chinese partners, which is standard for foreign-invested mining enterprises in China. The regulatory relationship with local and provincial authorities in Hebei has been broadly constructive, and the company has maintained its operational licence on a continuing basis. That said, Chinese mining law and foreign investment rules can shift, and regulatory changes represent a material variable that investors must track.

Revenue is derived from the sale of gold dor bars, zinc concentrate, and silver. Gold is sold at prevailing market prices, typically via Chinese domestic channels or international offtake agreements. Zinc concentrate is sold to Chinese smelters under long-term offtake arrangements. The split between gold and zinc revenue has fluctuated with commodity prices, but zinc typically contributes 25-40% of total revenue depending on the zinc price environment.

Recent Catalysts

  • Gold price strength: Gold has traded at multi-year highs through Q1 2026, with spot prices holding above USD2,800/oz. This is a direct earnings tailwind for every ounce Griffin produces. The relationship between the GFM share price and the gold price has been imperfect -- the stock has not fully tracked the commodity, suggesting persistent undervaluation relative to the gold cycle.
  • Zinc price recovery: Zinc has been among the better-performing base metals through 2025-2026, supported by supply discipline and improving Chinese industrial demand. As a meaningful revenue line at Caijiaying, zinc prices above USD3,000/tonne materially enhance the by-product credit and compress the effective cost of gold production. This is a variable that is systematically underweighted by investors who categorise Griffin as a gold stock.
  • Hebei regulatory stability: The Hebei provincial government has maintained a constructive posture toward the Caijiaying operation. No material regulatory or permitting issues have emerged in recent periods, which provides a degree of comfort for a single-asset Chinese mining operation.
  • UK small-cap commodity re-rating: The broader UK small-cap mining space has seen improving sentiment through early 2026 as gilt yields stabilise and gilt-equity correlation normalises. Griffin Mining has not participated fully in this recovery, which leaves room for catch-up re-rating as the market digests the multi-commodity earnings profile more accurately.

Key Risks

  1. China country risk (HIGH): This is the primary risk variable for Griffin Mining. Every ounce of gold, every tonne of zinc, and every ounce of silver is produced in China. Chinese mining regulation, foreign investment policy, resource nationalism, provincial-level permitting, and the broader geopolitical relationship between China and the West all represent variables that can affect the operating environment. Beijing's posture toward foreign-owned mining operations has historically been constructive, but the direction of travel on resource sovereignty is worth monitoring closely. Any material shift in Hebei provincial policy or central government direction toward foreign mining interests would be a significant negative catalyst.
  2. Single-asset concentration (HIGH): Caijiaying is Griffin Mining -- there is no second asset of material significance. Any geological underperformance, mechanical failure, permitting delay, or operational disruption at Caijiaying flows directly and fully to the company's earnings and cash flow. The company does not have a development pipeline that could offset production issues in the medium term. This single-asset risk is structural and permanent until and unless a second material asset is acquired or developed.
  3. Commodity price exposure (MEDIUM): Gold and zinc prices are the two primary earnings drivers. Gold represents the larger revenue component, but zinc is the variable that most investors underappreciate. A sustained fall in either gold below USD2,500/oz or zinc below USD2,500/tonne would compress margins and free cash flow materially. The company does not appear to maintain an active hedging programme, meaning full exposure to spot price movements.
  4. UK small-cap liquidity risk (MEDIUM): With a market capitalisation of approximately GBP150m, Griffin Mining has limited daily trading liquidity. This constrains institutional ownership to mandates that can accommodate sub-GBP200m positions. The bid-offer spread can be wide, particularly in periods of market stress. Any large selling pressure can generate meaningful price impact.
  5. Currency exposure (MEDIUM): Griffin reports in GBP but earns revenue in USD (gold) and RMB (zinc sold into China). RMB weakness against GBP compresses reported earnings from the Chinese zinc operation. USD/GBP is the dominant currency risk on gold revenues. A sustained move in either exchange rate can materially affect reported earnings in either direction.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Investors who want gold price exposure with a value tilt -- the 8x earnings multiple is genuinely cheap for a producing precious metals asset. Those who understand and are comfortable with China operating risk as a known, priced variable. Investors who want zinc by-product optionality without buying a pure zinc miner. UK small-cap specialists who can tolerate liquidity risk in exchange for unconstrained upside. Those who want a diversified commodity portfolio that is not purely gold-dependent.

Should avoid: Investors who require geographic diversification in their commodity exposure -- China is the only jurisdiction, and any China-specific risk event flows directly to GFM. Growth investors who need a development pipeline narrative. Those who cannot tolerate single-asset risk in a small-cap mining company. Anyone who requires a large, liquid, institutionally owned stock. Investors who need their gold exposure to be denominated in USD or held in a North American-listed vehicle.
