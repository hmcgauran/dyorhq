Executive Summary

Valero is a high-quality, geographically advantaged Gulf Coast refiner that has delivered strong shareholder returns, but at 31.5x trailing earnings the market is pricing in a much better refining environment than currently exists - the risk-reward does not justify new capital at this level, and REDUCE is the appropriate recommendation.

Business Model

Valero is the second-largest US refiner by throughput capacity, operating 15 refineries with combined crude processing capacity of approximately 3.2 million barrels per day. The asset base is predominantly Gulf Coast and Midcontinent, with major complexes in Texas (Houston, Texas City, Corpus Christi, Three Rivers), Louisiana (St. Charles, Meraux), and Oklahoma (Ardmore). This Gulf Coast concentration provides structural advantages in accessing heavily discounted Permian Basin and Gulf of Mexico crude oil.

Valero's refineries are among the most complex in the world - the Texas City and St. Charles refineries in particular can process heavy sour crude oils (including Canadian heavy oil via rail and pipeline) that are significantly discounted to light sweet crudes. This crude diet flexibility is a genuine competitive advantage and enables Valero to capture margins that less-complex competitors cannot.

The ethanol segment (Valero's renewable fuels business) produces approximately 1.6 billion gallons of ethanol annually from its 12 ethanol plants. This diversifies the product slate and provides exposure to corn price movements. The renewable diesel business is growing in importance as compliance with RFS2 (Renewable Fuel Standard) mandates creates demand for biomass-based diesel.

The retail network of approximately 7,400 branded wholesale and 2,100 retail sites (primarily in Texas, Oklahoma, and Louisiana under the Valero, Diamond Shamrock, and Beacon brands) completes the vertical integration. Capital allocation has been aggressive: Valero has been a heavy buybacker and dividend payer, returning over $10 billion to shareholders in aggregate since 2020.

Recent Catalysts

  • The key story for Valero over 2024-2025 has been the Gulf Coast crack spread advantage relative to other US regions. Permian crude pipeline constraints have kept Gulf Coast crude discounts elevated, benefiting Valero's Texas and Louisiana refineries which are configured to run heavily on Permian-sourced feedstocks. This structural advantage has been more durable than many expected.
  • Ethanol margins have been volatile - corn price spikes in 2024 hurt ethanol profitability, but the RFS2 compliance market has provided a floor for renewable diesel revenues. Valero's ethanol segment provides diversification but also adds exposure to agricultural commodity prices.
  • Valero has been the most aggressive buybacker in the refining peer group - the company repurchased approximately $3.5 billion in shares in 2024, reducing shares outstanding by approximately 4-5%. At current prices this is materially accretive and the primary value creation mechanism.
  • Dividend of approximately $4.28/share at current prices yields approximately 1.8% - lower yield than peers, which reflects management's preference for buybacks over dividends as the primary capital return mechanism.
  • Crude prices have moderated from 2022-2023 peaks to a $65-$80 range, which is broadly supportive for refining economics but has normalised crack spreads from their exceptional 2022-2023 levels. The "normal" for crack spreads post-2023 is meaningfully lower than the 2022-2023 average.

Thesis Evaluation

Bull Case (30% weight)

Crude demand firm; aviation and industrial diesel demand exceed expectations; Permian logistics constraints persist, maintaining the Gulf Coast crude discount that Valero exploits. Crack spreads re-accelerate. Ethanol segment benefits from strong gasoline demand. EPS approaches $12+ and shares re-rate to 25x earnings = $300+. Total return of 30%+.

Base Case (50% weight)

Crude in $65-$80 range; crack spreads moderate at current levels; Gulf Coast structural advantage maintained. EPS in the $8-$10 range for FY2026. Share buybacks continue reducing count. Shares trade $220-$260 at 25-28x earnings. HOLD warranted; total return of 8-12% including dividend, primarily from buybacks.

Bear Case (20% weight)

Mild US recession; crack spreads compress below cash breakeven for high-cost assets; Middle East/Asia new refining capacity creates structural oversupply; corn price spike hurts ethanol. Shares fall to $130-$150 at approximately 15x depressed earnings - rough historical floor for Valero given asset quality.

Key Risks

  1. Refining margin normalisation: The 31.5x P/E is pricing in a sustained crack spread environment that does not currently exist. The extraordinary margins of 2022-2023 have normalised, and any structural increase in global refining capacity (particularly Middle East additions) would compress margins structurally. The multiple is simply too high relative to normalised earnings for a cyclical business.
  2. Crude oil price and slate risk: Valero's complex refineries are configured for heavy sour crude. A rapid shift in North American crude production toward lighter, sweeter slates (which cannot be processed as efficiently at Valero's complex units) would impair the feedstock cost advantage. While the Permian is still dominant, any acceleration in Bakken or DJ Basin production would alter the crude slate dynamics.
  3. Ethanol segment volatility: Ethanol margins are highly sensitive to the corn/crude oil price ratio. A sustained corn price spike (as seen in 2024) can turn the ethanol segment into an earnings drag. This is a diversification that cuts both ways.
  4. Long-term petrol demand destruction: Long-term EV penetration in Texas (Valero's home market) is lower than California but will accelerate. The Gulf Coast refineries are harder to repurpose than some competing assets. The 10-15 year demand outlook for petrol is more challenged than for diesel.
  5. Capital intensity and environmental compliance: Valero's legacy asset base requires ongoing environmental capital expenditure, particularly given EPA regulations on refinery emissions. Compliance costs are substantial and rising, potentially limiting free cash flow available for buybacks in future years.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Owners: Value-oriented energy investors who want a high-quality refining operation with strong free cash flow; those who bought significantly below $200 and are holding with a long time horizon; investors who want to hold the energy sector through a crude cycle and believe current crack spread levels are sustainable.

Avoiders: Growth investors; those who want a pure energy transition story; anyone who believes the 31.5x P/E is pricing in too much good news; ESG-focused investors concerned about long-term petrol demand; investors looking for dividend yield (PE is too high to be competitive as a yield vehicle).

Recommendation

OPPORTUNISTIC BUY

Valero is a high-quality refining business with genuinely advantaged assets and strong management. The problem is entirely one of price. At 31.5x trailing earnings on $7.58 EPS, the market is pricing in a sustained crack spread environment that does not currently exist and that is not justified by the structural outlook for global refining capacity. The bull case requires crack spread re-acceleration that, while possible, is not the base case. HOLD would be warranted at $180-$200; at $238, there is insufficient margin of safety for a new position.

Entry framework: BUY below $165 (approximately 20-22x normalised EPS), which would represent reasonable value for a high-quality refiner with Valero's asset quality and cash generation. At that level the dividend yield becomes meaningful and the risk-reward improves substantially. BUY on a sustained break above $255 as a momentum signal. Over 12 months, base-case price target $255 assuming $10 EPS and a 25x multiple - modest upside from $238.

Stop-loss: A sustained break below $200 would suggest crack spread deterioration and a loss of the Gulf Coast structural advantage, warranting a full reassessment of the thesis. At that level the case for cutting losses becomes compelling.
