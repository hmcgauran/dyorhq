Executive Summary

Critical Mineral Resources PLC is a UK AIM micro-cap explorer with no revenue, negative equity, and no JORC-compliant resource. Its only material asset is the Agadir Melloul copper-silver project in Morocco, where drilling is underway ahead of a maiden JORC resource estimate targeted for Q3 2026. The investment case is entirely binary — a successful resource confirmation could re-rate a sub-GBP 10M company; failure is a total loss event for equity holders. With severe CLN dilution overhang, going concern qualifications, and imminent assay results (April 2026), CMRS is appropriate only as a sub-0.5% speculative position. SELL at 22/100 conviction.

Business Model

CMR is an early-stage copper and critical mineral exploration company building a portfolio of near-term development assets in Morocco — a jurisdiction it considers mining-friendly, well-located to serve European markets, with attractive permitting and fiscal conditions. The company operates through its 80%-owned Moroccan subsidiary Atlantic Research Minerals SARL (ARM), acquired in July 2023.

The principal asset is the Agadir Melloul copper-silver project in Morocco. Diamond drilling commenced in early 2026 following an oversubscribed fundraising. Visible copper sulphide and oxide mineralisation has been confirmed in Zones 1 and 2, but no JORC-compliant resource estimate has been published. CMR has stated an internal exploration target of 150,000–200,000 tonnes of contained copper equivalent at approximately 1.2% CuEq, defined across less than 5% of the total licence area — a figure that is conceptual in nature and may not be realised. The company also holds earlier-stage projects including the Hesperis portfolio and high-grade silver Igli Project, plus a nascent metals trading business. These are not material to the investment case.

Key Risks

  1. Financial distress and going concern (CRITICAL): FY2024 audit included a material going concern uncertainty. Cash was GBP 70,073 against liabilities of GBP 519,107. Post-year-end financing provides near-term liquidity, but with no revenue and no JORC resource, further equity raises are certain. Each raise dilutes existing holders. A failed drilling outcome makes subsequent fundraising extremely difficult.
  2. Binary exploration outcome (HIGH): There is no JORC resource at Agadir Melloul. The internal exploration target of 150,000-200,000t CuEq at 1.2% CuEq is conceptual. Actual resources could be materially lower, higher, or non-existent. A failed or subscale drill programme is a total loss event for equity investors.
  3. Convertible note dilution overhang (HIGH): Multiple CLN tranches convert at 1.1p-1.45p per share — significantly below the current 2.24p price. Forced conversion will materially dilute existing shareholders. The GBP 2.5M Gilini facility also carries equity conversion features at low prices.
  4. Development funding gap (HIGH): A JORC resource is only the first step. Building a mine requires hundreds of millions of dollars. CMR, at sub-GBP 10M market cap, cannot access capital markets at viable terms to fund development. The company needs a strategic partner, offtake prepayment, or acquisition — all uncertain and potentially highly dilutive.
  5. AIM micro-cap illiquidity (HIGH): Average daily volume of ~1.4 million shares translates to only approximately GBP 31,000 per day in value. In a stressed market, the spread on AIM micro-caps is extreme. Exiting a meaningful position without significant price impact is unrealistic.
  6. Exploration stage slippage (MEDIUM): The DFS is targeted for December 2026, but junior mining projects routinely slip. Metallurgical test work, process design, EIA submissions, and environmental studies all remain outstanding. Any delay extends the period of financial vulnerability.
  7. Commodity price exposure (MEDIUM): Copper prices fluctuate. While the structural deficit narrative supports higher long-term prices, a sustained fall in the copper price would impair project economics and the financing options available to CMR.
  8. Jurisdiction risk (MEDIUM): Morocco is considered mining-friendly and stable, but regulatory changes — including royalty increases, restrictions on foreign ownership, or permitting delays — would directly impact project viability.
