Executive Summary

Palantir is one of the few listed companies that can credibly claim to be both a data analytics platform and an AI beneficiary. Its Gotham and Foundry platforms sit directly in thepath of US government intelligence and defence spending, and the commercial Foundry product has been expanding meaningfully into enterprise AI use cases. At $143.88, the stock is down 7.9% from its 52-week high of $156.20 — a modest pullback from AI-hype peaks — but the valuation of 221× earnings leaves zero margin for error on execution.

The government AI thesis is Palantir's strongest bull case: the US defence and intelligence establishment is explicitly prioritising AI adoption, and Palantir's data integration platform is deeply embedded in those systems. The commercial AI case is real but less differentiated — Snowflake, Databricks, and hyperscaler AI platforms all compete for enterprise data workloads.

Bottom line: HOLD. Government AI is a genuine market and Palantir has the relationships and platform to win it. But 221× P/E demands continued outperformance. Trim above $150.

Business Model

Palantir sells two core platforms. Gotham is used by US and allied government intelligence and defence agencies for data integration, analysis, and operational intelligence. Foundry is the commercial equivalent, selling data integration and analytics to enterprise customers. AIP (Artificial Intelligence Platform) is the next-generation product integrating large language models into both Gotham and Foundry, positioning Palantir as an AI infrastructure provider for organisations that need AI capabilities integrated with their existing data.

Revenue split is approximately 55% government, 45% commercial. Government contracts are typically multi-year and sticky — once deployed, the switching cost is extremely high. Commercial growth is the swing factor for the investment case.

The competitive moat is the data integration layer — Palantir's ability to ingest, unify, and query disparate structured and unstructured data at scale. This is genuinely hard to replicate, especially in government environments where security clearance and procurement complexity create additional barriers.

Recent Catalysts

  • AIP launch momentum: AIP has been the primary commercial growth driver, as enterprises adopt Palantir's data platform as the infrastructure layer for LLM-based AI applications. This is a credible product-market fit for government and enterprise alike.
  • US government AI spending: The Department of Defense and intelligence community have explicitly prioritised AI adoption in FY2025–2026 budgets. Palantir's embedded position in existing government systems is a structural advantage — the incremental cost of adding Palantir AI capabilities to an existing contract is much lower than winning new business.
  • Commercial momentum: Foundry has been gaining traction beyond the original target market of defence-adjacent commercial customers, expanding into financial services, healthcare, and energy. This broadens the commercial growth case.
  • Post-election defence budget: Any increase in US defence spending is a direct tailwind for Palantir's government revenue. The current administration's stated commitment to AI in national security is a structural positive.

Key Risks

  1. 1Valuation risk: 221× earnings is the dominant risk. Any guidance miss — on revenue, margins, or commercial growth — would cause immediate derating. The market is paying for flawless execution.
  2. 2Government budget concentration: ~55% of revenue from US government creates political and fiscal risk. A defence budget cut or programme cancellation would disproportionately impact Palantir.
  3. 3Commercial competition: Snowflake, Databricks, and hyperscaler AI platforms all compete for enterprise data and AI workloads. Palantir's differentiation is real but not insurmountable, and enterprise buyers are increasingly price-sensitive.
  4. 4AIP commercial execution: AIP must convert from promising product to durable commercial revenue. Early traction is encouraging, but repeatable, scalable commercial sales require different capabilities than government work.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Investors who want specific government AI exposure with a proven product and sticky government contracts. Those who believe AIP will drive the next leg of commercial growth. Long-term investors who can hold through quarterly volatility.

Should avoid: Value investors. Anyone who thinks the AI premium is excessive. Investors who need predictable earnings growth quarter-to-quarter.
