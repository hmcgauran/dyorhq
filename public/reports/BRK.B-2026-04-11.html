Executive Summary

Berkshire Hathaway is a conglomerate holding company with dominant insurance operations and a disciplined capital allocation philosophy; its intrinsic value growing at above-market rates and significant liquidity make it a compelling long-term hold at $479.90.

Business Model

Berkshire operates a diversified conglomerate with two core segments. Key revenue streams:

  • Insurance underwriting: GEICO, reinsurance (General Re, BRK B reinsurance), and other specialty insurers. Float-funded investments generate investment income.
  • Insurance investment income: Float from insurance operations is invested in equities, bonds, and cash — a significant and growing earnings driver.
  • Railroad (BNSF): One of North America's largest freight railroads, generating stable, capital-intensive cash flows.
  • Utilities (Berkshire Hathaway Energy): Regulated and unregulated energy businesses providing reliable long-term earnings.
  • Manufacturing, Services & Retail: Wide-ranging operations from Precision Castparts to Dairy Queen — diverse, often cyclical.
  • Apple and equity portfolio: Significant minority stakes in publicly traded companies, particularly Apple (~$150bn+ position).

The genius of the model is the insurance float — capital collected before claims are paid — deployed at Berkshire's discretion into higher-returning assets.

Key Risks

  1. Equity market drawdown: ~$400bn+ of the investment portfolio is in publicly traded equities; a 30% market correction would reduce reported book value significantly.
  2. Succession risk: Post-Buffett, the investment and capital allocation decisions — arguably the most critical ingredient in Berkshire's value — face an execution gap.
  3. Catastrophe risk: As a major reinsurer, large natural catastrophes or man-made losses can create large, sudden underwriting losses.
  4. Concentration risk: Apple alone represents ~40% of the equity portfolio; a drawdown in Apple disproportionately affects reported results.
  5. Overpayment risk: Berkshire's size makes it difficult to deploy capital at attractive rates; acquirers of last resort risk overpaying in competitive processes.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Own It

  • Long-term investors seeking a diversified, conservatively managed holding company that compounds capital reliably.
  • Those who want equity-like returns with bond-like downside discipline — Berkshire rarely overpays and often buys back shares opportunistically.
  • Investors who trust the insurance float model and Berkshire's ability to deploy massive cash at above-market rates.

Avoid It

  • Investors needing income — no dividend.
  • Short-term traders — the stock is a compounding machine, not a trading vehicle.
  • Those expecting Berkshire to always deploy cash aggressively — management's discipline is a feature, not a bug, but means periods of apparent inactivity.
