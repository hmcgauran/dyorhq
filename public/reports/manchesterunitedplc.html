Executive Summary

Manchester United is the world's most widely recognised football brand, a asset-based equity story where the core asset is not earnings but the Old Trafford franchise, the global commercial reach built over 147 years, and the structural uplift arriving via the 2025-26 Premier League broadcast deal. INEOS's minority takeover in early 2024 ended the dysfunctional Glazer dynasty and introduced cost discipline for the first time in over a decade. The club is loss-making at the EPS level, debt is material at approximately GBP 650 million, and there is no near-term path to profitability given the investment required in the playing squad under a new manager. Yet at USD 17.92, the stock trades below even the bear-case scenario, pricing in a full reversal failure.

The equity is not an earnings play. It is a call option on a successful INEOS rebuild, on Champions League qualification, and on the broadcasting and commercial tailwinds arriving in 2025-26. The binary nature of the thesis — INEOS rebuild succeeds or it does not — means conviction is structurally capped. But the option is not expensive at current levels.

Bottom line: SPECULATIVE BUY at USD 17.92. The stock is not a buy at this price, but it is not a sell either. Wait for a pullback below USD 16.00 to add, or for concrete on-pitch evidence of the Amorim rebuild bearing fruit before building a position. The new Premier League TV deal is a structural revenue catalyst that will benefit every club, including Manchester United, from 2025-26 onwards.

Business Model

Manchester United generates revenue across three principal streams. Matchday revenue covers ticket and matchday hospitality sales at Old Trafford (capacity circa 74,000), which remains one of the largest club stadiums in Europe. Broadcasting revenue derives from Premier League domestic and international TV rights distributions, UEFA competition participation fees, and related media income — this stream is highly sensitive to final league position, with Champions League qualification worth substantially more than Europa League or mid-table finishes. Commercial revenue encompasses the Adidas kit partnership (a ten-year agreement worth over GBP 900 million from 2015, with renegotiation terms under review), the Snapdragon front-of-shirt sponsorship (from 2023), TeamViewer's training ground naming rights, and broader merchandise and licensing income.

The critical structural feature of the business model is its operating leverage to league position. A club finishing fourth versus twelfth in the Premier League can see hundreds of millions of pounds of difference in annual broadcasting income, given the new 2025-26 TV deal structure. This makes the commercial and broadcasting revenue streams effectively cyclical, not recurring, in practice. INEOS has sought to dampen this by cutting the wage bill and reducing the scouting network, but the primary variable remains on-pitch performance.

Recent Catalysts

  • INEOS minority takeover (early 2024): Jim Ratcliffe's INEOS acquired a controlling stake from the fractured Glazer family after years of deadlock. INEOS invested GBP 200 million-plus and effectively took de facto control of football operations. Omar Berrada (ex-Manchester City) installed as CEO. Dan Ashworth appointed sporting director. This was the most significant governance event in the club's history since the 2005 Glazer leveraged buyout.
  • Ruben Amorim appointed manager (November 2024): A multi-year commitment to a manager with a coherent tactical system. Amorim's Sporting CP record suggests he can develop a competitive squad. His appointment marks the end of the interim manager era and the beginning of a structured rebuild. The 2024-25 season is the first full season under his system.
  • New Premier League TV deal (2025-26 season): The next domestic and international broadcast rights cycle delivers a 25-30% increase in total Premier League TV revenue. This is a structural tailwind for all clubs, regardless of performance. Manchester United's overseas broadcast share remains tied to final league position, but the base floor rises materially.
  • INEOS cost-cutting programme: Significant reductions in the wage bill, scouting network, and IT infrastructure. This is a structural positive for profitability if maintained and if it does not materially damage squad quality. The test of this discipline will come when the squad requires reinvestment.
  • No Champions League football in 2024-25: A material broadcasting revenue headwind versus recent prior years. This is a known and discounted item, but it keeps earnings pressure on for the current fiscal year.

Thesis Evaluation

Bull Case (25% weight)

INEOS rebuild delivers. Amorim's system beds in and the squad returns to Champions League qualification within two years. The 2025-26 TV deal compounds the revenue base. Adidas and Snapdragon deals renew favourably, demonstrating that commercial partners still value the brand at the highest levels. INEOS commits to a stadium renovation, unlocking additional matchday revenue. Net debt stabilises and begins to decline. EPS approaches breakeven by 2026-27. Price target USD 28-32.

Base Case (55% weight)

Club stabilises as a consistent Europa League qualifier. INEOS cost discipline delivers gradual margin improvement without catastrophic squad degradation. The new TV deal offsets the structural absence of Champions League revenue in 2024-25. Amorim is a solid if unspectacular Premier League manager. EPS remains negative but improves. The stock drifts in a USD 20-24 range as the market waits for evidence of a sustained on-pitch recovery. Price target USD 20-24.

Bear Case (20% weight)

INEOS cuts go too deep, damaging squad quality irreparably. Amorim's system fails to translate to the Premier League. The club misses Champions League for five or more consecutive seasons. Commercial partners use non-qualification as leverage to renegotiate terms downward. Old Trafford investment decision forces a dilutive capital raise or further debt. Debt remains elevated. The Glazer structural governance problems resurface. Price target USD 12-16.

Key Risks

  1. On-pitch performance binary (HIGH): The single largest driver of revenue — broadcasting income — is entirely dependent on league position. A club that finishes fourth versus twelfth in the Premier League can earn hundreds of millions of pounds more in broadcast revenue. This is not a risk that can be hedged or diversified away. It is the core of the investment thesis and its opposite.
  2. Debt structure and financial flexibility (HIGH): Net debt of approximately GBP 650 million is a persistent structural concern. The Glazer era leveraged buyout saddled the club with debt that has been a recurring overhang. INEOS has not yet articulated a clear deleveraging path. Any stadium investment — a genuine long-term need — could add GBP 2 billion or more in additional capital requirement.
  3. INEOS cost-cutting overshoot (MEDIUM): The aggressive reduction in the wage bill, scouting network, and support functions is a calculated bet. If it impairs squad quality and the club fails to qualify for Champions League for a prolonged period, the commercial and broadcast revenues will follow the on-pitch decline. The cost cuts will have been counterproductive.
  4. Stadium investment decision (MEDIUM): Old Trafford requires material investment — renovation or new build estimated at GBP 2 billion-plus. INEOS has signalled willingness to invest but the decision is outstanding. A dilutive equity raise or additional debt to fund this would be structurally negative for existing shareholders.
  5. Share class structure and governance (MEDIUM): MANU (Class B) carries no voting rights. Class A shares (MANUA) hold voting rights. INEOS controls via preference shares and arrangements with the Glazer family. Minority Class B holders have limited governance influence over major decisions including stadium investment, capital raises, or ownership changes.
  6. Commercial partner renegotiation risk (LOW-MEDIUM): If the club's sporting trajectory continues to disappoint, Adidas and Snapdragon have commercial leverage to renegotiate terms at the next renewal opportunity. The current deals are structured to be valuable, but that value depends on continued top-tier sporting exposure.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Investors with a genuine conviction that INEOS can restore Manchester United to Champions League regular status within three years. Those who understand that football clubs are asset-based, hard-to-value investments where brand and franchise value can diverge from earnings for extended periods. Investors who can tolerate binary outcomes and a multi-year holding horizon. The stock is appropriately sized as a satellite position in a broader portfolio.

Should avoid: Value investors — the absence of earnings and the presence of significant debt makes traditional valuation metrics meaningless. Income investors — there is no dividend and no near-term path to one. Short-horizon investors — this is a multi-year thesis with no guaranteed catalyst timeline. Anyone who cannot stomach a 30-40% drawdown if the Amorim rebuild stalls and the club misses Champions League again in 2025-26.

Recommendation

SPECULATIVE BUY at USD 17.92. This is not a buy. It is not a sell. The equity trades at a meaningful discount to even the bear-case scenario, reflecting the market's justified scepticism about the INEOS rebuild given the track record of managerial and executive failure since Sir Alex Ferguson retired. That discount may be appropriate. But it also means the optionality is asymmetric: the downside from current levels is partially protected by brand value and the structural TV deal tailwind, while the upside remains significant if Amorim and INEOS deliver.

Do not build a meaningful position above USD 19.00. The 52-week high of USD 19.65 represents near-term resistance and is not a compelling entry point for a binary turnaround story. Wait for pullbacks below USD 16.00, or for concrete on-pitch evidence (league position, cup runs, transfer market activity) before initiating.
