Executive Summary

United Airlines is a high-quality global network carrier with a compelling international growth trajectory and a P/E under 10x that significantly undervalues its earnings power and asset base, and is rated HOLD.

Business Model

United is one of the world's largest airlines, operating a comprehensive global network from its hubs at major US cities. Following the 2022 announcement of "United Next", the carrier has committed to a significant fleet transformation aimed at improving unit economics and competitive positioning:

Mainline Passenger Revenue~85% of revenue; domestic and international scheduled service
Cargo~5% of revenue; belly cargo on passenger aircraft and freighter operations
Mileage Plus (Loyalty)~7% of revenue; co-brand credit card partnership and retail monetisation
ancillary / Other~3%; ground handling, maintenance services

United's "United Next" plan involves retiring older narrowbody aircraft and replacing them with a large order of Boeing 737 MAX 10s and Airbus A321neos, which offer significantly better fuel efficiency and passenger experience. The international network -- particularly the Pacific and Atlantic routes -- is United's key differentiator versus low-cost carriers and a major driver of premium revenue.

Q4 2025 Earnings Beat: United reported adjusted EPS of $2.85 in Q4 2025, significantly ahead of the $2.40 consensus estimate, driven by strong international premium demand and continued domestic capacity discipline. Full-year 2025 EPS reached $11.50.

Pacific Network Expansion: United launched three new Pacific routes in early 2026 (San Francisco-Shanghai, Los Angeles-Tokyo Haneda, and Denver-Singapore), capitalising on its unique Polaris business class product and capturing corporate travel demand that has shifted from Asian carriers.

Mileage Plus Valuation: United's loyalty programme was independently valued at $22-26 billion in a recent analyst note, providing significant embedded value relative to the current enterprise value of the entire airline.

Boeing 737 MAX 10 Delivery Delay: Boeing continues to delay 737 MAX 10 certification and deliveries, creating fleet transition risk for United's "United Next" plan; the carrier has publicly acknowledged this and is evaluating contingency plans, including the potential substitution of A321neo orders.

Key Risks

#RiskDescription
1Boeing fleet transition riskUnited has bet heavily on the 737 MAX 10 for its narrowbody fleet renewal. Prolonged Boeing delivery delays would increase operating costs on older aircraft and limit capacity growth, directly impacting earnings.
2International demand deteriorationUnited's premium valuation depends on sustained transatlantic and transpacific corporate travel demand. A China economic slowdown or recession in key European markets would disproportionately affect United versus low-cost domestic carriers.
3Fuel price spikeFuel is the largest cost line for any airline; a 20-25% increase in jet fuel prices without ticket price pass-through would erode United's margins significantly given the already thin airline economics.
4Labour cost escalationPost-pandemic pilot and crew contracts have significantly raised labour costs across the industry. Further increases in the upcoming contract negotiations could add $500-800 million in annual costs.
5Operational executionUnited has a history of operational reliability challenges. A high-profile operational failure, IT outage, or safety incident would disproportionately damage the premium brand equity that underpins the investment thesis.
