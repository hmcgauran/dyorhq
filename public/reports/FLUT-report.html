Business Model

Flutter Entertainment is the world's largest online gambling company by revenue, operating a portfolio of distinct brands across sports betting, casino, poker, and daily fantasy sports (DFS). Its US flagship, FanDuel, holds the number-one position in US online sports betting by gross gaming revenue and is the group's primary vehicle for the North American growth story. In Europe and internationally, Flutter runs Paddy Power (UK and Ireland), Betfair (UK, notable for its peer-to-peer exchange model), and PokerStars — the global dominant brand in online poker. The group holds gambling licences across more than 20 US states and a broad footprint across Europe, Australia, and parts of Latin America, making it one of the most geographically diversified operators in the sector. Products range from pre-match and in-play sportsbook wagering to casino slots and table games, tournament and cash-game poker, and DFS contests. Revenue is generated through three distinct mechanisms. Sportsbook revenue accrues from the overround — the margin embedded in odds offered to customers — which represents the difference between implied probability and true probability; Flutter books this as settled revenue once bets are resolved. Poker and casino generate rake (a percentage fee on each pot in poker) and game margin (the built-in house edge on casino products). Betfair's exchange model is structurally different: the group takes a commission on winning bets placed on its peer-to-peer marketplace rather than acting as counterparty to every wager, which means it carries no sportsbook risk on exchange volume. Subscription and DFS revenues add a smaller, more recurring income stream. Across all products, the group's trading function — its ability to manage odds risk in real time — is the primary determinant of margin quality. The US is the dominant growth narrative and the dominant valuation problem simultaneously. Legalised online sports betting remains an early-stage market: fewer than half of US states have authorised it, implying a long runway as remaining states legislate. FanDuel's market leadership gives it genuine scale advantages in brand recognition and customer acquisition efficiency. However, the market has attracted fierce competition from DraftKings, BetMGM, Caesars, and ESPN Bet, all spending heavily on marketing and promotional offers, driving customer acquisition costs (CAC) well above what early bull-case models assumed. State-by-state regulatory risk — including tax rate increases, advertising restrictions, and in-play betting constraints — compounds the uncertainty. By contrast, the European business (Paddy Power, Betfair UK/Ireland) is mature and stable, generating low-growth cash flows from well-entrenched brands in saturated markets. The derating from $313 to $102 — roughly 87% — is broadly justified by the structural deterioration in the US thesis. When the stock peaked, the market was pricing a rapid expansion of the US addressable market and early profitability at scale. Neither has materialised on schedule: the pace of state legalisation has slowed, promotional intensity has compressed sportsbook margins industry-wide, and FanDuel's path to the EBITDA margins originally modelled remains elusive. FanDuel's leading US position is real but the competitive moat is narrower than the market share ranking implies — DraftKings in particular has shown willingness to sustain large losses to gain share, which keeps the entire sector's profitability in question. At 10–12× forward EBITDA, the stock is no longer priced for perfection, but the combination of CAC inflation, regulatory headwinds, and persistent promotional pressure creates a credible bear case below $90. The REDUCE recommendation reflects a balance of the long-term US opportunity against near-term earnings risk and the absence of clear near-term catalysts for re-rating.
