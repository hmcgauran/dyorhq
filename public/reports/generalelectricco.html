Executive Summary

GE Aerospace is one of the great industrial turnarounds of the decade. The 2025 FY results delivered record EPS with total orders of $27 billion (+74% year-on-year), driven by commercial engine demand from Airbus and Boeing's production ramp. The LEAP engine continues to gain market share and the services business provides enormous recurring revenue. The spin-off to GE Vernova (energy) and GE HealthCare has left a cleaner, higher-quality business. At 37.9x P/E on record earnings, the valuation is premium — but the aerospace backlog is multi-decade in duration and the business has genuine pricing power. OPPORTUNISTIC BUY reflects the quality but acknowledges the stock is within 11% of its 52-week high, limiting the near-term upside and requiring patience for a better entry.

Business Model

GE Aerospace designs, manufactures, and services jet engines, turboprop, turbofan, and turboshaft engines for commercial and military aircraft. Its two core franchises are the LEAP engine (co-produced with Safran, capturing roughly 60% of the narrowbody market) and the GE9X, the exclusive powerplant for the Boeing 777X. The commercial engines business sits within a large and highly defensive aftermarket services operation — airlines cannot fly without certified engines, creating sticky, high-margin recurring revenue that typically doubles the profit contribution of the original equipment sale.

The spin-offs of GE Vernova (energy) and GE HealthCare have sharpened the group's focus entirely on aerospace. The remaining business benefits from unrivalled R&D scale, a backlog measured in decades of production, and structural pricing power as the sole or dominant supplier in several engine programmes. Defence revenue provides cyclical ballast, though it is a smaller proportion of total sales than at some peers.

Recent Catalysts

The 2025 full-year results confirmed GE Aerospace as a structurally transformed business. Orders of $27 billion represented a 74% year-on-year increase, reflecting accelerating demand from both Airbus and Boeing as narrowbody production rates are stepped up. The LEAP engine programme is performing at full production tempo, with unit counts supporting multi-decade aftermarket revenue streams. The GE9X received certification momentum for the 777X, removing a key technical risk from the investment case.

On the operational side, management has maintained strict cost discipline through the production ramp, protecting margins despite supply chain pressures. The balance sheet is now fortress-grade following years of deleveraging, giving the company flexibility to return capital to shareholders while funding continued R&D. The dividend was reinstated and has grown, though the yield remains modest given the premium valuation.

Thesis Evaluation

Bull Case (75)

Commercial aerospace recovery continues on its current trajectory. LEAP and GE9X engine orders accelerate as Airbus and Boeing hit their stated production rate targets. Services revenue grows 8-10% annually as the installed base matures into high-margin aftermarket. The market re-rates GE Aerospace to 42-45x P/E, reflecting the quality and duration of the backlog. Price target: $380-$420.

Base Case (55)

The aerospace backlog supports steady 10-15% revenue growth over the forecast period. EPS settles in the $7.00-$8.50 range as production rates normalise and aftermarket revenues build. The market assigns a P/E of 40-45x, sending the stock to $340-$370 as investors await the next catalyst (defence contract wins, LEAP market share expansion). This is the most probable outcome at current prices.

Bear Case (30)

Boeing production rates disappoint again due to factory quality issues or supply chain fractures. Airline customers slow or delay orders, putting pressure on the engines business. GE Vernova residual liabilities and warranty costs bleed into parent-level perception. The market de-rates to 28-32x earnings as growth visibility diminishes. Price falls to $195-$230, testing the 52-week low.

Key Risks

  1. Boeing and Airbus production execution: GE Aerospace's fortunes are tightly linked to OEM production rate ambitions. Any sustained shortfall at either Boeing or Airbus filters directly into engine order cancellations or deferrals.
  2. LEAP engine competitive dynamics: The LEAP is a joint venture with Safran. Any shift in the competitive balance — such as Pratt & Whitney recovering market share — could affect programme economics and aftermarket pricing.
  3. GE Vernova residual liabilities: Although formally separated, GE Aerospace retains some legacy exposure to GE Vernova's energy transition costs and warranty obligations. Any large unexpected charge at Vernova could weigh on the parent's narrative.
  4. Defence budget pressure: A significant portion of GE Aerospace's defence revenue is tied to US and NATO defence spending. Budget sequestration or political gridlock could reduce long-term visibility on military engine programmes.
  5. Valuation premium risk: At 37.9x P/E, GE Aerospace prices in considerable execution perfection. Any disappointment relative to elevated expectations could trigger a sharp de-rating given the limited near-term upside to the 52-week high.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Owners: Long-term investors seeking quality industrial exposure with a multi-decade backlog visibility. Aerospace sector specialists who understand the aftermarket revenue model and are comfortable with premium valuations. Those who already own GE near $250-$270 should hold; the cycle has years to run.

Avoiders: Value investors will struggle with the P/E multiple. Short-term traders seeking a catalyst near current levels should wait. Those who believe Boeing's operational problems represent a systemic risk to the supply chain should remain underweight or absent.

Recommendation

OPPORTUNISTIC BUY. GE Aerospace is a high-quality business in an industry with structural tailwinds. The backlog is measured in decades, the aftermarket revenue is sticky and high-margin, and the management team has demonstrated execution discipline through a difficult restructuring. At 37.9x P/E, the stock is not cheap — but the quality justifies a premium.

Entry framework: BUY below $270 (implies approximately 33x P/E, a reasonable margin of safety for a premium-quality industrial). ADD below $250 (provides 10-15% upside to base case with limited downside risk to bear case). TRIM above $320 (valuation stretched relative to base-case earnings power; take some profit and redeploy on any meaningful pullback).
