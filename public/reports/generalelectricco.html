Business Model

GE Aerospace designs, manufactures, and services jet engines, turboprop, turbofan, and turboshaft engines for commercial and military aircraft. Its two core franchises are the LEAP engine (co-produced with Safran, capturing roughly 60% of the narrowbody market) and the GE9X, the exclusive powerplant for the Boeing 777X. The commercial engines business is amplified by a large and highly defensive aftermarket services operation — airlines cannot fly without certified engines, creating sticky, high-margin recurring revenue that typically doubles the profit contribution of the original equipment sale.

The spin-offs of GE Vernova (energy) and GE HealthCare have sharpened the group's focus entirely on aerospace. The remaining business benefits from unrivalled R&D scale, a backlog measured in decades of production, and structural pricing power as the sole or dominant supplier in several engine programmes. Defence revenue provides cyclical ballast, and the $190 billion backlog ($20 billion growth YoY) gives extraordinary revenue visibility.

Services revenue grows 8–10% annually as the installed base matures into high-margin aftermarket. The market re-rates GE Aerospace to 42–45x P/E, reflecting the quality and duration of the backlog. Base Case (55)

The market assigns a P/E of 40–45x, sending the stock to $340–$370 as investors await the next catalyst (defence contract wins, LEAP market share expansion). GE Vernova residual liabilities and warranty costs bleed into parent-level perception. The market de-rates to 28–32x earnings as growth visibility diminishes. Key Risks

  1. Boeing and Airbus production execution: GE Aerospace's fortunes are tightly linked to OEM production rate ambitions. Any sustained shortfall at either Boeing or Airbus filters directly into engine order cancellations or deferrals.
  2. LEAP engine competitive dynamics: The LEAP is a joint venture with Safran. Any shift in the competitive balance — such as Pratt & Whitney recovering market share — could affect programme economics and aftermarket pricing.
  3. GE Vernova residual liabilities: Although formally separated, GE Aerospace retains some legacy exposure to GE Vernova's energy transition costs and warranty obligations. Any large unexpected charge at Vernova could weigh on the parent's narrative.
  4. Defence budget pressure: A significant portion of GE Aerospace's defence revenue is tied to US and NATO defence spending. Budget sequestration or political gridlock could reduce long-term visibility on military engine programmes.
  5. Valuation premium risk: At 37.9x P/E, GE Aerospace prices in considerable execution perfection. Any disappointment relative to elevated expectations could trigger a sharp de-rating given the limited near-term upside to the 52-week high.
