Executive Summary

Cohort PLC is a multi-disciplined UK defence and security group operating through four distinct subsidiaries: MASS (defence electronics and electronic warfare), EID (military communications), SOE (sonar and acoustic systems), and ASV (autonomous systems). The group sits beneath the UK Government's elevated defence spending commitment, benefiting from structural budget allocation to electronic warfare, maritime surveillance, and autonomous capabilities - all areas where Cohort holds established positions with the UK MOD, European defence ministries, and NATO-aligned customers.

At approximately 18x forward earnings for a growing defence business with visible contract revenue and sovereign capability arguments, CHRT trades at a meaningful discount to comparables. The UK defence budget is ring-fenced in an era of fiscal constraint, and Cohort's positioning across EW, sonar, and autonomous systems maps directly to stated capability priorities. The primary risk is scale: at ~£280m market cap, the stock lacks the liquidity and analyst coverage of larger defence names, and growth is highly dependent on a handful of large contract awards.

Bottom line: BUY (strong). The valuation is undemanding for a business with Cohort's contract backlog and exposure to structurally growing defence categories. Accumulate on any weakness related to timing delays or sector derating.

Business Model

Cohort operates as a holding company for four defence technology subsidiaries, each with distinct capabilities and customer relationships. This structure provides revenue diversification across capability domains while allowing operational autonomy.

MASS (Defence Electronics): The largest subsidiary by revenue. Specialist in electronic warfare (EW) systems, surveillance, and countermeasures. MASS supplies the UK MOD and export customers with EW suites, communication intelligence systems, and sensor packages. Long-standing supplier relationships and security-cleared workforce represent a meaningful barrier to entry.

EID (Military Communications): Portuguese-headquartered military communications specialist. Designs and supplies tactical communications systems, including vehicle-mounted and soldier-worn equipment, to European and Middle Eastern defence ministries. Accretive geographic diversification to the group.

SOE (Sonar and Acoustic Systems): Specialist in submarine and surface-ship sonar systems. Supplies the UK Royal Navy and international navies with acoustic sensors, torpedo detection systems, and underwater surveillance capabilities. The maritime domain is a stated UK and NATO investment priority.

ASV (Autonomous Systems): Designs and supplies autonomous maritime vessels and unmanned surface vehicles (USVs). Growing exposure to the autonomous warfare theme, including participation in UK Navy autonomous trials. Smaller but fast-growing division.

Revenue is predominantly contract-based with the UK MOD and export defence ministries. Contracts range from multi-year framework agreements to smaller technical services engagements. Export revenue provides geographic diversification and reduces UK budget concentration risk.

Recent Catalysts

  • UK Integrated Review refresh: The 2023 Integrated Review committed to 2.5% of GDP on defence and stated commitments to maritime, cyber, and autonomous capabilities. Cohort's divisions map directly to these priorities. Further policy clarity in 2025-2026 continues to reinforce the investment thesis.
  • Electronic warfare budget uplift: NATO allies are accelerating EW investment following lessons from contemporary conflict zones. MASS's EW capabilities position it to benefit from UK and export programme expansion. The UK MOD's Land EW programme and associated sensor upgrades are ongoing pipeline opportunities.
  • Maritime domain investment: The Royal Navy's Dreadnought submarine programme and continued surface fleet investment support sonar demand at SOE. Submarine and anti-submarine warfare capabilities are a stated NATO priority in the current strategic environment.
  • Autonomous systems momentum: ASV's participation in UK Navy trials and growing interest from allied navies in USV capabilities represents a nascent but real growth vector. The autonomous warfare theme is early-stage but gaining budget traction.
  • EID contract wins: EID's Portuguese and European customer base continues to generate repeat orders for tactical communications upgrades. Geographic diversification away from UK budget dependency is a structural positive.

Thesis Evaluation

Bull Case (25% weight)

Large EW programme awards from UK MOD drive MASS revenue significantly higher. SOE wins a major sonar export contract with an allied navy. ASV secures a substantial USV order, establishing it as a meaningful revenue contributor. CHRT re-rates toward 22x as defence premium pricing increases. Price target 1,800p+.

Base Case (55% weight)

Steady organic growth across all four divisions. UK MOD contract flow remains stable. EID maintains its European customer base. ASV grows modestly. Revenue growth of 5-8% annually with margin maintenance. P/E holds at 17-20x. Price target 1,400-1,600p.

Bear Case (20% weight)

UK fiscal tightening forces MOD budget reprioritisation, delaying Cohort contract awards. A major programme is re-scoped or delayed. Competitive pressure from larger defence primes on export deals. CHRT de-rates to 13-15x on reduced growth expectations. Price target 900-1,100p.

Key Risks

  1. UK MOD budget concentration (HIGH): A significant portion of group revenue is derived from UK MOD programmes. Any meaningful reduction in UK defence spending - whether from fiscal pressure, policy shift, or programme reprioritisation - would directly impact Cohort's top line. The ring-fenced defence budget commitment has political risk in a changing government environment.
  2. Export approval dependency (MEDIUM): Export contracts to Middle Eastern and European customers require government-to-government approval. Political relationships and export licensing create uncertainty and delay risk that Cohort cannot control.
  3. Contract timing volatility (MEDIUM): Large defence contracts are episodic. A gap between major programme completions and new awards can cause meaningful revenue lulls. This timing risk is structural to the business and not fully visible in reported order books.
  4. Scale and liquidity (MEDIUM): At ~£280m market cap on AIM, CHRT lacks the analyst coverage, liquidity, and institutional investor base of larger defence names. The spread can be wide and large positions are difficult to build or exit without market impact.
  5. Competition from primes (MEDIUM): Larger defence primes (BAE Systems, Babcock, Leonardo) compete for the same UK MOD programmes and export contracts. Cohort's subsidiaries compete as tier-2/3 suppliers, which provides some protection but also limits the contract sizes available to them.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Investors seeking direct exposure to the UK defence spending uplift without the large-cap premium. Those who want to add defence exposure alongside BAE Systems and Babcock with a higher-beta, higher-conviction position. Investors who understand AIM liquidity dynamics and can size positions appropriately. Long-term holders who accept the episodic nature of contract awards in exchange for the structural tailwind.

Should avoid: Investors who require daily liquidity in their defence allocation. Those who are uncomfortable with revenue concentration risk in UK MOD programmes. Anyone expecting consistent quarterly earnings growth - defence contracting is inherently lumpy. Investors who need regular analyst coverage and corporate broker support (CHRT on AIM has limited sell-side attention).
