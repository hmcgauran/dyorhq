Executive Summary

American Express's premium consumer and corporate franchise continues to compound earnings reliably, but the shares trade near the top of their 52-week range at 20x P/E with credit normalisation risk on the horizon - OPPORTUNISTIC BUY is the weighted recommendation.

Business Model

American Express operates a closed-loop payments network - unlike Visa and Mastercard, which facilitate transactions between banks, Amex is itself the issuer and the network. This allows it to capture the full economics of each transaction: interchange fees from merchants, interest income from cardholder revolving balances, and annual fee income from premium card products.

The franchise is centred on premium and super-premium consumer cards (Platinum, Gold, Centurion/Lounge Access) and corporate expense management solutions (American Express Business). The model is characterised by high cardmember spending per account, strong annual fee revenues, and a relatively affluent, low-credit-loss customer base. The LTM (Lending <-> Marketing) initiative seeks to more aggressively grow revolving balances - a potential EPS accelerator but one that increases credit risk.

Merchant discount revenue (the fee merchants pay to accept Amex) accounts for approximately 40% of total revenues. Network revenues, annual fees, and interest income make up the remainder.

Recent Catalysts

American Express has continued to add affluent cardmembers, with membership growing at a mid-single-digit rate even as the broader credit card market has normalised. B2B payments growth (American Express Business) has been a highlight, as the company takes share from traditional corporate card providers. The Zelle integration and expanded Blue Bird merchant acquisition have supported merchant discount revenue. However, credit loss ratios have been rising from COVID-era lows - net charge-offs have normalised to more historical levels - and net interest income growth has moderated as the rate cycle peaks. The stock has retreated from its 2024 highs as growth decelerates from extraordinary post-COVID levels.

Thesis Evaluation

Bull Case (78)

Premium consumer spending remains resilient. B2B payments growth accelerates as Amex takes corporate card share. LTM initiative generates meaningful revolving balance growth without a credit deterioration. EPS grows to $18.50+. Price: $375+.

Base Case (66)

Cardmember spending grows mid-single digits. Credit normalises with net charge-offs returning to historical averages (~2.5-3%). NII growth moderates as Fed holds rates. EPS grows to $16.00-$16.80. Price: $315-$350.

Bear Case (42)

Consumer credit deteriorates as excess savings from the pandemic fully unwind. Charge-offs rise above historical averages, forcing provisions. The LTM initiative fails or produces elevated credit costs. Merchant acceptance disadvantage intensifies. EPS falls to $13.00-$14.00. Price: below $260.

Key Risks

  1. Consumer credit normalisation: Amex's affluent customer base has historically had low credit losses, but the post-COVID normalisation is not yet complete. If charge-offs rise significantly above historical norms, provision expenses will suppress earnings growth.
  2. Net interest income cycle sensitivity: Amex is a significant NII contributor as it grows revolving balances. The rate environment is pivotal: if the Fed cuts rates faster than expected, NII compresses and acts as a headwind to EPS growth.
  3. Merchant discount rate competitive pressure: Amex's premium merchant fees have historically been higher than Visa/Mastercard, leading some merchants (particularly in SMB) to restrict acceptance. Continued merchant pushback could limit acceptance breadth and indirectly affect cardmember value.
  4. B2B payments competitive intensity: Capital One's acquisition of Discover and other bank card issuers are competing aggressively for corporate card share. Brex, Divvy and other fintech corporate card challengers are gaining traction in the SMB segment.
  5. Macro consumer spending slowdown: As a premium consumer franchise, Amex is exposed to affluent consumer sentiment. A US recession disproportionately affects the upper-income consumer spending that drives Amex's billing volumes.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Owners: Quality-conscious financial sector investors seeking premium franchise exposure with a strong brand moat. The 20x P/E is reasonable for the earnings quality and consistent capital return track record. Long-term holders who have ridden the post-COVID premium spending boom.

Avoiders: Investors seeking value in financials will prefer bank issuers trading at lower P/Es. Those expecting a consumer credit deterioration should reduce or avoid. The near-52-week-high price limits short-term upside.

Recommendation

HOLD. American Express is a high-quality, premium-franchise payments company with a genuine moat in affluent consumer and corporate spending. The 20x P/E is fair but not cheap relative to the risks. The credit normalisation story is the primary near-term earnings variable. Patient investors should hold and watch charge-off trends carefully.

Entry framework: BUY below $275 (implies 18x P/E - attractive relative to quality). Reduce on rallies above $355 (implies 23x P/E and pricing in full credit optimism). Monitor net charge-off trends quarterly as the primary risk signal.
