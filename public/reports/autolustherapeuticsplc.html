Executive Summary

Autolus Therapeutics received FDA approval for obecabtagene autoleucel (brand name AUCATZYL) in November 2024 and has spent the first year of commercial launch building momentum. The company reported USD 75 million in Year-1 net product revenue and raised full-year 2025 figures in January 2026, with 2026 guidance of USD 120-135 million. This is a solid commercial start for a niche CAR-T therapy in a competitive and small indication. However, the stock has drifted 40 per cent below its 52-week high of USD 2.70 as investors worry about competition from Gilead/Kite's Tecartus, slower than expected centre activation, and the broader biotech funding environment. At USD 1.61 the market is signalling that the launch is not going well enough to justify the current burn rate and cash runway.

Bottom line: REDUCE - SPECULATIVE at USD 1.61. The commercial launch is real and the revenue trajectory is positive, but the competitive dynamics and cash burn rate create a more binary risk-reward than the current price implies. Progress oncentre activation and real-world evidence data will be the next catalyst.

Business Model

Autolus earns revenue from sales of AUCATZYL to authorised treatment centres in the United States. The therapy is used for adult patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (r/r ALL), a rare blood cancer with approximately 4,000-6,000 new cases per year in the United States, of which roughly 20-25 per cent are eligible for third-line CAR-T treatment. The commercial model relies on a network of haematology centres that undergo a certification process to handle the therapy, which has a 20-30 day vein-to-vein delivery window. Pricing is set at approximately USD 510,000 per infusion, consistent with the broader CAR-T class.

Autolus's pipeline includes next-generation programmes using its proprietary CAR-T technology platform targeting other haematological malignancies. These are earlier-stage and do not yet contribute to revenue.

Key Risks

  1. Competition from Tecartus: Gilead's Tecartus has first-mover advantage, broader label, and a larger sales infrastructure. Autolus must demonstrate meaningful differentiation on safety or efficacy to take share, not just earn a niche.
  2. Cash burn and financing risk: With negative EPS and quarterly cash burn of approximately USD 35-45 million, the company has finite runway. A secondary offering within 18 months is highly probable and will be dilutive.
  3. Centre activation rate: Revenue growth is directly tied to how quickly haematology centres complete the Autolus certification process and begin ordering. Delays in activation are the primary source of guidance risk.
  4. Binary event sensitivity: Clinical data readouts, FDA decisions, or partnership announcements create dramatic moves in either direction given the low share price and high volatility (beta 2.04).

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Specialist biotech investors with high risk tolerance who understand the CAR-T commercial landscape and can monitor centre activation metrics as a leading indicator.

Should avoid: Generalist investors, anyone needing earnings visibility or positive cash flow, and those unwilling to tolerate a potential dilutive financing within 18 months.
