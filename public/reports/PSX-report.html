Executive Summary

Phillips 66 is a high-quality downstream refining and marketing business that has delivered substantial shareholder returns through cycles, but with refining margins moderating from 2022-2023 peaks and the shares re-rated at 14.8x earnings, the bull case is largely played out — HOLD is the appropriate stance, with conviction scores insufficient to warrant new buying at this level.

Business Model

Phillips 66 is a major US downstream energy company spun off from ConocoPhillips in 2012. The business is organised into three segments: R refining and Marketing (R&M): The core business, operating 13 refineries across the US with total crude processing capacity of approximately 2.9 million barrels per day. The refinery system is geographically advantaged, with a high proportion of coastal assets (including the 330,000 bpd Los Angeles refinery and the 150,000 bpd San Francisco-area refinery) that benefit from constrained West Coast gasoline crack spreads. The R&M segment transforms crude oil into refined products — petrol, diesel, jet fuel, and petrochemicals — and sells these through Phillips 66, 76, and Conoco branded retail sites (approximately 7,600 outlets across the US). Midstream (including CPChem): Phillips 66 owns 50% of Chevron Phillips Chemical (CPChem), a major olefins and polyolefins producer. This joint venture provides a higher-growth, higher-margin chemicals platform and partial diversification from pure refining. Specialty Lubricants and Other: The company produces and markets lubricants, basestocks, and waxes under brands including Phillips 66 Lubricants and Kendall. Capital allocation has been aggressive: the company has returned significant cash via dividends and buybacks, and reduced leverage following the 2023 disposal of its Western Europe refining network to bp. At current crude prices and crack spread levels, the model generates substantial free cash flow.

Recent Catalysts

The sale of the company 65% interest in the San Francisco Bay Area refinery (including the Rodeo facility) toRhodes for $500 million+ closed in mid-2025, completing a multi-year portfolio rationalisation. Combined with the 2023 Western Europe refining disposal to bp, this significantly reduced the refining footprint and shifted the portfolio toward higher-quality, higher-margin coastal assets. Crude oil prices have moderated from 2022-2023 peaks, settling into a $65-$80 range. This is broadly supportive for refinery margins as it reduces feedstock cost pressure while refined product demand remains firm. However, diesel and jet fuel crack spreads have normalised from their 2022-2023 peaks, meaning the extraordinary refining profitability of that period has normalised. Share buybacks have been significant — Phillips 66 has been aggressively repurchasing shares, having bought back approximately $4 billion in 2024. This is mathematically accretive at current prices relative to intrinsic value. Dividend of approximately $4.40/share represents a 2.7% yield at current prices — meaningful but not the primary return driver. The Trump administration's tariffs on imported petroleum products have been a topic of policy discussion in 2025-2026, with potential implications for crack spreads at coastal refineries — net effect is modestly positive for Phillips 66 given its US-heavy asset base.

Key Risks

Refining margin cyclicality: The 2022-2023 period was exceptional for US refiners due to supply chain disruptions and Russian refinery outages. The normalisation of crack spreads from those peaks is t

  • 1Refining margin cyclicality: The 2022-2023 period was exceptional for US refiners due to supply chain disruptions and Russian refinery outages. The normalisation of crack spreads from those peaks is the most important risk to the bull thesis. Any structural increase in global refining capacity (particularly new Middle East and Asia capacity additions planned for 2025-2027) would compress margins structurally.
  • 2Crude oil price volatility: A sharp crude price spike (above $100) would compress refinery margins as product prices lag. A crude price collapse would be temporarily negative for refining throughput economics. The company's hedging programme mitigates but does not eliminate this risk.
  • 3Leverage and capital intensity: Though the company has reduced leverage following asset sales, the refining business is capital-intensive. Environmental regulatory compliance (especially in California and Washington state) requires ongoing capital expenditure on refinery upgrades. If capital expenditure requirements accelerate faster than free cash flow, buybacks could be curtailed.
  • 4Energy transition risk: Long-term demand destruction for petrol-powered transport would be structurally negative for petrol demand. Phillips 66's West Coast assets face the highest electrification risk in the medium term (California EV penetration rates are the highest in the US).
  • 5Geopolitical tariff uncertainty: Refined product trade flows are globally integrated. Any significant US tariff escalation disrupting trade flows could create crack spread volatility that is difficult to predict and position for.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Owners: Value-oriented energy investors who want downstream refining exposure; those who bought in below $120 and are holding on to the thesis; investors seeking energy sector exposure with high and growing dividends. Cyclical investors who want to hold through a refining cycle and believe current margins have further to normalise. Avoiders: Growth investors; those looking for earnings acceleration; investors who believe crude prices will fall materially; anyone unwilling to hold through refining margin cyclicality. Long-term ESG-focused investors concerned about long-run transport fuel demand destruction.

Recommendation

HOLD — Conviction Score: 61/100 Phillips 66 is a well-run, cash-generative refining business trading at a reasonable 14.8x trailing earnings. The asset portfolio rationalisation (European and select US asset sales) has improved the quality of the remaining refining system. However, the extraordinary earnings power of the 2022-2023 refining cycle has moderated, and the shares now trade at a fair-but-not-compelling multiple for a business with limited structural growth drivers. The buyback programme is the primary value creation mechanism at current prices. Entry framework: BUY if shares fall below $120 (approximately 11-12x normalised EPS), which would represent genuine undervaluation relative to the asset base and sum-of-parts. Alternatively, a sustained break above $185 would be a momentum BUY signal reflecting crack spread re-acceleration. Target $180-$200 in a bull scenario over 12-18 months; base-case target $160 in a 12-month horizon. Stop-loss: A sustained break below $120 would suggest fundamental deterioration in the refining margin environment or a more serious competitive threat. At that level, the dividend yield becomes the primary attraction, not capital appreciation.
