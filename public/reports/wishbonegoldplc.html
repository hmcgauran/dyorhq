Executive Summary

Wishbone Gold PLC is a listed gold explorer and mining company focused on West Africa, a region with significant geological potential but meaningful political, operational, and liquidity risk. The company is loss-making with negative EPS and trades at a distressed valuation, well below its 52-week range. The near-term investment case rests entirely on exploration success and the ability to fund operations without catastrophic dilution -- a binary outcome unsuitable for most institutional investors. HOLD. Conviction Score: 35/100 - HOLD

Business Model

Wishbone Gold PLC is a junior precious metals explorer operating in West Africa, with project interests in Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire -- two of Africa's most established mining jurisdictions. The company's model is characteristic of the junior mining peer group: financed by equity raises, progressing exploration assets through the resource pipeline from greenfield drilling to measured resource definition, with the ultimate exit being either a major mining company acquisition or development of a producing asset. Revenue is currently negligible; the company is entirely dependent on capital markets to fund exploration activity. The West African geological belt is well-established -- multi-hundred-ounce gold deposits are documented across Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire -- but geological promise does not guarantee commercial discovery, and junior explorers at this stage carry a very high failure rate. The company has historically sought to diversify into broader natural resources including base metals, which introduces execution risk across multiple commodity cycles.

Key Risks

  1. #1 Exploration binary risk: Junior mining at this stage carries an extremely high failure rate. A failed or sub-economic drilling programme can wipe out the investment thesis entirely, with no floor on the share price given thin liquidity.
  2. #2 Capital markets dependency: The company is loss-making and must raise equity periodically. Each raise is dilutive. If gold prices fall or market conditions deteriorate, the company may be unable to fund its exploration programme, creating existential risk.
  3. #3 West African political and regulatory risk: Ghana and Cote d'Ivoire, while more stable than many African jurisdictions, carry residual risks of resource nationalism, permit revocation, tax regime changes, and community conflict over land use.
  4. #4 Liquidity and shareholder base risk: Thin daily volume and a constrained shareholder register mean that institutional investors cannot size a position without moving the market significantly. This also means exit risk is material -- the bid-offer spread and price impact make disciplined risk management difficult.
  5. #5 Commodity price exposure: While gold is currently elevated, a sustained decline in the gold price would reduce the attractiveness of the entire West African gold exploration sector, impairing both the company's financing options and its potential acquisition by a strategic buyer.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It
