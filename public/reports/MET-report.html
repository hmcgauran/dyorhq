Executive Summary

MetLife is a high-quality life insurance franchise with a diverse global product mix and an attractive dividend yield, but structural headwinds in Japan life insurance and variable annuity markets limit the re-rating potential, and the stock offers a balanced but uninspiring risk-reward at current levels — HOLD.

Business Model

MetLife is one of the world's largest life insurance companies, serving approximately 100 million customers across more than 40 countries. Its core products include life insurance (term, whole life, universal life), annuities (fixed, variable and indexed), employee benefits (group life, disability and dental) and asset management. MetLife operates in six segments: US, Japan, Asia, Latin America, Europe/Middle East/Africa and MetLife Holdings. The company benefits from the demographic tailwind of aging populations requiring retirement income products, particularly in Japan and the United States. Earnings are supported by fee income from annuities and employee benefits, as well as a substantial investment portfolio (the float) that generates investment income. The dividend has been progressive and well-covered.

Recent Catalysts

MetLife has been navigating a challenging interest rate environment: higher rates improve investment income on the float but also increase the competitive pressure from bank retirement products and can increase surrenders in variable annuity products with guaranteed living benefits. The Japan business has been relatively stable but faces a low interest rate environment that limits the profitability of traditional life insurance products. In the US, the employee benefits business (voluntary benefits and group insurance) has been a steady contributor. Management has been returning capital through buybacks and dividend increases. The stock is down from its 52-week high of $83.85 but is above its 52-week low of $67.33.

Key Risks

  • 1Variable annuity guarantee risk: MetLife has a substantial block of VA contracts with guaranteed living benefits. In a prolonged low-rate environment or equity market downturn, the cost of maintaining these guarantees could be significant.
  • 2Japan interest rate sensitivity: Japanese life insurance profitability is closely tied to the interest rate environment. The Bank of Japan's policy trajectory remains uncertain, and prolonged low rates would structurally compress margins in MetLife's largest non-US market.
  • 3US employment cycle risk: The employee benefits segment is directly correlated with US employment levels. A recession that raises unemployment would reduce covered lives and premium volumes.
  • 4Regulatory and accounting changes: Changes to insurance capital regulations (ICS) or US GAAP accounting for long-duration insurance contracts could require reserve increases that impair reported earnings.
  • 5Competition in retirement and income products: Banks, asset managers and annuity competitors are all competing aggressively for the retirement savings market. Product innovation and pricing pressure in annuities could limit growth in one of MetLife's key earnings streams.
