Business Model

Ford operates across three primary vehicle segments following its organisational restructuring:

  • Ford Blue (ICE): Traditional internal combustion engine vehicles — F-Series trucks, Mustang, Explorer, Bronco — the cash-generating backbone of the company. Ford Blue generates the profits and free cash flow that funds the EV transition. The segment is in structural long-term decline as consumer preferences shift.
  • Ford Model e (EVs): Electric vehicles including the F-150 Lightning (electric truck), Mustang Mach-E, and E-Transit van. This segment is currently loss-making and is expected to remain so until EV scale reaches the level required to achieve manufacturing cost parity with ICE vehicles.
  • Ford Pro (Commercial): A dedicated commercial vehicle and fleet services business selling ICE and EV commercial vehicles globally, alongside telematics and service contracts. This is a high-margin, sticky business that benefits from the transition to electric commercial fleets.
  • Ford Credit: The captive finance arm provides auto financing and leasing to Ford customers and dealers. It provides a steady earnings contribution and helps support vehicle sales through promotional leasing programmes.

Ford's core strategic challenge is managing the simultaneous decline of its ICE cash cow and the losses of its EV growth engine — a transition that has proven far more difficult and expensive than originally modelled.

Recent Catalysts (Last 3–6 Months)

  • EV loss expansion: Ford Model e losses have continued to expand, with the segment reporting EBIT losses of $4.7bn in fiscal 2025 — significantly worse than the $2.3bn loss in fiscal 2024 and far exceeding the $1.5bn originally guided. The path to EV profitability remains elusive.
  • Chinese OEM competitive threat: BYD and other Chinese manufacturers have entered the global EV market with vehicles priced 30–50 per cent below comparable Ford models, creating existential pricing pressure on Ford's EV lineup in international markets.
  • UAW contract headwind: The 2023 United Auto Workers strike settlement added approximately $900 per vehicle in labour costs — a structural margin headwind that makes Ford's US manufacturing less globally cost-competitive just as EVs require heavy capital investment.
  • ICE truck pricing pressure: US full-size truck pricing has softened as the post-COVID demand surge fades and GM and Ram compete aggressively for truck market share. F-Series margins are compressing from peak levels.
  • Strategic restructuring charges: Ford has announced significant restructuring charges including plant closures and workforce reductions in Europe and North America, signalling management acknowledgement that the current cost structure is unsustainable.

Key Risks

1EV transition cash burn: Ford Model e is burning billions of dollars annually without a clear path to breakeven. The longer the transition takes, the more ICE cash flow is consumed, potentially depleting the financial reserves needed to fund the transformation before it is complete.
2Chinese OEM competition: BYD, Geely, SAIC, and other Chinese manufacturers have achieved manufacturing cost leadership in EVs that Ford cannot replicate in the near term without dramatically higher volumes. If Chinese EVs flood the US market following tariff relief, Ford faces structural price disadvantage.
3ICE margin compression: The F-Series and core ICE vehicles are Ford's financial foundation. Competitive pressure from Ram, GM Silverado, and Japanese manufacturers, combined with industry-wide incentive escalation, is compressing the margins that fund the EV transition.
4Labour cost structure: The UAW 2023 contract adds approximately $900 per vehicle in costs and creates a challenging competitive position versus non-unionised manufacturers and international brands with lower labour cost structures.
5Technology and software execution: Ford's in-vehicle software (SYNC 4/5) and advanced driver assistance systems (BlueCruise) lag Tesla's FSD and Autopilot in consumer perception. As vehicles become software-defined platforms, this technology gap could become a meaningful competitive disadvantage.

Avoid If

  • Seeking any quality automotive franchise — GM has managed the EV transition more efficiently and with less capital destruction, making it a structurally superior holding in the sector
  • Cannot tolerate negative earnings and dividend cuts — Ford is loss-making today with significant uncertainty about the timing and magnitude of a recovery
  • Long-term fundamental investors who prefer Tesla's vertical integration strategy or pure EV plays over a managed transition with large legacy liabilities
