Executive Summary

Business Model

Humana is one of the largest US Medicare Advantage (MA) managed care organisations, with approximately 5.3 million medical plan members as of FY2025. The core business model involves contracting with the US federal government (Centres for Medicare and Medicaid Services, CMS) to provide health benefits to Medicare-eligible seniors under MA plans — these are Medicare Advantage Prescription Drug (MAPD) and standalone Prescription Drug Plan (PDP) products. Premium revenues are received from CMS (risk-adjusted, based on member demographics and health status) and from member premiums. The MA model is capitated: Humana receives a fixed per-member per-month (PMPM) premium from the government, bears the medical cost risk, and earns the difference between premium revenues and medical costs incurred. This creates strong incentives for care management, chronic disease management, and utilisation control — Humana's CarePoint (proprietary provider engagement platform) and clinical programmes are designed to manage this risk while improving member outcomes. The key revenue metrics are MBR (medical benefit ratio, the inverse of margin), Stars ratings (CMS quality bonus programme), and membership growth. Higher Stars ratings (4+ stars) unlock quality bonus payments that are material to the economics — at 4-star level, a health plan receives a 5% quality bonus on base premium; at 5-star level it can receive 5% plus a 3% quality bonus. Approximately 66% of Humana's members were in 4+ star plans as of the last reporting period, but Stars maintenance is increasingly difficult as CMS raises the bar. Humana also operates a commercial segment (employer group plans) and a military/Veterans health contract (TRICARE), but Medicare is the dominant business by membership and earnings.

Recent Catalysts

Humana's share price has declined significantly from the $315 high set in early 2025, falling nearly 40% to the current $192 area. This sell-off reflects legitimate concerns: MBR deterioration in the Medicare business, competitive pressure from UnitedHealth and regional MA plans, and uncertainty about the Stars ratings cycle. Medical cost trends in Medicare Advantage have been elevated —Utilisation has increased post-COVID as seniors who deferred care during the pandemic returned to the healthcare system. This has pressured MBRs across the MA industry, and Humana has not been immune. FY2025 MBR guidance has been revised, creating earnings uncertainty. CMS released updated Stars cut scores for 2025-2026 measurement years, raising the bar on certain HEDIS quality metrics. This has raised concern that Humana's existing 4-star plans could be at risk of downgrading to 3.5 stars, which would trigger loss of quality bonuses — a direct earnings headwind estimated at $300-500 million in annual revenue at risk. The MA industry faces regulatory scrutiny from CMS on prior authorisation and benefit design. New CMS rules on prior authorisation have been implemented, and while these apply more directly to providers and other payers, the overall regulatory tone toward MA plans has become more adversarial under the current administration. Trump administration executive orders on Medicaid and Medicare spending have introduced political risk for the entire government programme healthcare sector. While MA is technically voluntary for seniors, any perception that CMS is cutting MA benefits or reimbursement rates would be a significant sector headwind. Humana has been investing heavily in care quality and Stars improvement initiatives (including the $1 billion+ Health Care Services platform investment), but the return on this investment has been slower than hoped.

Key Risks

  • 1Medicare Advantage reimbursement and Stars ratings risk: This is the dominant near-term risk. CMS sets MA reimbursement rates annually via the Advance Notice and Final Rate Notice. Any net negative rate change (or higher-than-expected benefit cut) would directly impair Humana's earnings. Similarly, any Stars downgrade directly reduces quality bonus income, which can be worth $2-3 per share in earnings. The dual risk of rate pressure and Stars downgrades simultaneously is the worst-case scenario for MA investors.
  • 2Medical cost ratio (MCR) inflation: Post-COVID utilisation has remained elevated. Humana's MBR (inverse of margin) has deteriorated, and if it continues, earnings will miss guidance. The challenge is that seniors are deferring less care now that the pandemic is over, and chronic disease burden is growing — this is a structural headwind for the entire MA industry, not just Humana.
  • 3Competitive dynamics: UnitedHealth Group has been aggressively growing its MA membership, leveraging its scale and integrated care model (Optum). Regional players are also competing hard, and CVS/Aetna (through its Medicare business) is a significant competitor. This competitive intensity makes Stars maintenance harder and pricing discipline more difficult to maintain.
  • 4Political and regulatory risk: Any federal action to cut MA reimbursement rates, impose a maximum cost model, or increase regulatory burden on MA plans would be materially negative for the sector. This risk has increased under the Trump administration's cost-cutting posture.
  • 5Debt levels and capital return: Humana has been investing heavily in care platform infrastructure andStars improvement programmes while simultaneously navigating a challenging MBR environment. If MBR does not improve as management expects, the company may face a cash flow squeeze that constrains capital return. The current market cap of $23 billion reflects a company with limited financial flexibility.

Recommendation

REDUCE — Conviction Score: 46/100 Humana is a high-quality business with a genuine Medicare Advantage franchise serving a growing demographic, but the operating environment has deteriorated meaningfully. MBR is rising, Stars ratings are under pressure, and the regulatory backdrop is less supportive. While the 40% share price decline from highs is significant, it has not yet reached the level at which the risk-reward becomes compelling for new capital. The valuation at 19.5x trailing earnings is fair-to-elevated given the uncertainty in the earnings trajectory. Until there is clear evidence of MBR stabilisation, new positions should be avoided. Entry framework: BUY below $150 if MBR shows signs of stabilising and the market has priced in a worst-case regulatory scenario — this would represent genuine value for a high-quality MA franchise. BUY also if Stars ratings are maintained at 4+ and the stock re-rates to 17-18x base-case EPS ($170-180), which would be an attractive re-entry after the current sell-off. Target $240-$260 in 12-18 months on a bull-case EPS of $14 and a 20x multiple. Stop-loss: A sustained break below $150 would be a significant concern, suggesting either a catastrophic Stars downgrade or material regulatory reimbursement rate cuts beyond what is currently priced in.
