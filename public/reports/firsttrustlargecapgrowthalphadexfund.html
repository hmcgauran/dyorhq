Executive Summary

The First Trust Large Cap Growth AlphaDEX Fund (FTC) is an exchange-traded fund that seeks to replicate the performance of the S&P 500 AlphaDEX Growth Index using an enhanced indexing methodology. The fund applies quantitative factor screening to identify large-cap US equities exhibiting growth characteristics, rebalancing semi-annually. At $168.09, the fund trades near the upper end of its estimated 52-week range ($140-$200), reflecting elevated investor appetite for growth-factor exposure heading into mid-2026. The fund is appropriate as a core US equity satellite position for investors seeking growth-tilted large-cap exposure with the diversification benefits of an ETF wrapper. OPPORTUNISTIC BUY at current levels. Conviction Score: 58/100 - HOLD

Business Model

First Trust Portfolios, the fund sponsor, operates FTC as a single-factor enhanced index fund. The underlying S&P 500 AlphaDEX Growth Index applies a proprietary ranking methodology to the S&P 500 universe, selecting the top-scoring growth stocks based on: (1) price momentum, (2) earnings growth momentum, (3) return on equity, and (4) management change signals. The fund holds approximately 200-300 stocks across sectors, with a deliberate growth and momentum tilt relative to the cap-weighted S&P 500 benchmark. FTC distributes dividends quarterly, has an expense ratio of approximately 0.60-0.70% (confirmed against current First Trust ETF fee schedules), and tracks the index using physical replication. The fund's AlphaDEX methodology is distinct from passive cap-weighted indexing: it systematically overweights stocks with positive growth and momentum signals while underweighting those that score poorly on those dimensions. The result is a portfolio with higher turnover and higher sector active weights compared to a standard S&P 500 ETF, with performance that diverges materially in factor-driven market environments.

Recent Catalysts

US large-cap growth equities have been supported through early 2026 by resilient corporate earnings, a supportive Federal Reserve policy backdrop, and continued AI-capex-driven earnings upgrades for mega-cap technology names. FTC's growth-factor tilt means it carries significant sector overweights in technology, consumer discretionary, and communication services relative to a cap-weighted benchmark, positions that have benefited from the AI investment theme. The fund's semi-annual rebalance (typically in March and September) has recently overweighted stocks with strong earnings growth momentum, adding exposure to mid-cycle winners in semiconductors and cloud infrastructure. The fund does not issue press releases; key data points are limited to NAV updates, index reconstitution announcements, and ETF flow data. The current macro backdrop -- moderating inflation, a still-accommodative Fed, and solid consumer spending -- is broadly supportive for growth-factor performance.

Key Risks

  1. #1 Growth-factor reversal risk: FTC's systematic growth and momentum tilt means it can underperform significantly when factor leadership shifts away from growth toward value or quality, which can happen rapidly in late-cycle environments.
  2. #2 Interest rate sensitivity: Growth equities are structurally sensitive to changes in long-term interest rates. A sustained rise in the 10-year Treasury yield would compress present values of future earnings and disproportionately hurt FTC's portfolio holdings.
  3. #3 Concentration in mega-cap technology: FTC's AlphaDEX methodology tends to overweight the largest and most liquid growth stocks, resulting in significant implicit exposure to mega-cap technology names that already dominate the S&P 500, limiting true diversification benefit.
  4. #4 Turnover and tracking error: The semi-annual index rebalance generates portfolio turnover, creating transaction costs and potential tax inefficiency that erodes the net return advantage of the factor strategy versus a simple passive ETF.
  5. #5 Premium to net asset value: As an ETF, FTC trades at market price which can deviate from net asset value, particularly during periods of market stress or low liquidity, creating a execution risk not present in direct index investing.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Own: Investors seeking a growth-tilted US large-cap ETF with systematic factor exposure, as a satellite position to complement a core S&P 500 holding. Suitable for investors with a multi-year horizon who want to express a view on the persistence of growth-factor leadership. Avoid: Investors who prefer broad market-cap-weighted exposure at low cost (a simple S&P 500 ETF is likely superior on a risk-adjusted, cost-adjusted basis for most long-term investors). Investors with a short investment horizon or those who require precision on sector or factor timing. Those concerned about interest rate sensitivity should avoid growth-factor products at this stage of the cycle.
