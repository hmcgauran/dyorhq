Executive Summary

Duke Energy is a regulated utility operating across the Carolinas, Florida, Ohio, Indiana, and Kentucky; it offers a defensive 3.5% dividend yield supported by rate base growth from grid modernisation, but limited capital appreciation potential keeps us at HOLD.

Business Model

Duke Energy is a regulated electric and gas utility. Key revenue streams: Regulated Electric Utilities: Generates, transmits, and distributes electricity across six states. Revenue is approved by state utility commissions; recovery of costs plus an allowed return on regulated assets (rate base). Regulated Natural Gas: Pipeline and distribution operations in Ohio, Kentucky, and the Carolinas. Similar regulatory model with approved rates. Commercial Renewables: Growing but still modest portfolio of utility-scale solar and wind assets; unregulated but contracted under long-term PPAs. The regulated utility model is highly predictable: a state commission sets rates to allow recovery of prudent costs plus ~10% return on equity. Growth comes from rate base expansion — building new substations, smart meters, grid hardening, and new generation — approved through capital expenditure programmes.

Key Risks

  • 1Regulatory risk: Rate cases are the central earnings driver; adverse regulatory outcomes — denied investments, reduced rate base, or lower allowed returns — directly compress earnings growth.
  • 2Interest rate sensitivity: Utilities are bond proxies; a rising rate environment causes re-rating to lower P/E multiples. Also increases the cost of capital for regulated operations.
  • 3Load growth uncertainty: Decades of flat electricity demand are being disrupted by AI data centre power demand and industrial reshoring; the pace of change creates planning uncertainty.
  • 4Carbon policy risk: State-level clean energy mandates require significant capital investment; the pace of coal retirement creates stranded asset risk in older generation.
  • 5Hurricane and storm risk: Duke's service territory includes hurricane-prone areas (Carolinas, Florida); major storm events create restoration costs and regulatory recovery complexity.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Own It Income investors who need a 3.5% yield with high conviction of sustainability; utility dividends are among the most reliable in the S&P 500. Defensive portfolio positioning — utilities typically outperform in late-cycle and recessionary environments when growth slows. Long-term investors who want exposure to the grid modernisation and energy transition theme within a regulated, predictable structure. Avoid It Growth investors — limited capital appreciation; a utility is a yield vehicle, not a growth story. Those expecting rate cuts — lower rates make utilities less competitive versus bonds and typically cause multiple compression. Tax-inefficient accounts — utility dividends are taxed as ordinary income; tax-efficient accounts benefit from different structures.
