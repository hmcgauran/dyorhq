Executive Summary

Kering is the second-largest luxury goods group in the world, built around the Gucci franchise with supporting positions in Saint Laurent, Bottega Veneta, Balenciaga, and Alessandro Michele's legacy brands. At $21.92 (ADR), the stock is up modestly from recent lows but remains well below its 52-week high of $22.20, reflecting the market's continued uncertainty around Gucci's creative and commercial turnaround under new leadership.

Business Model

Kering generates revenue from owned brands across fashion, leather goods, jewellery, and watches. Gucci alone accounts for roughly half of group revenue. The group's strategy has been to shift toward fewer, higher-productivity directly operated stores while cutting wholesale exposure to protect brand equity and margin. The luxury goods moat is brand prestige combined with scarcity and heritage. Kering's challenge is that Gucci, its dominant brand, has been losing share to competitors — particularly LVMH's Louis Vuitton and Hermès — in the post-pandemic luxury boom. Gucci's reset under a new creative director is the central investment question. The group's other brands (Saint Laurent, Bottega Veneta) provide some diversification, but none individually compensates if Gucci underperforms. The portfolio strategy has shifted toward quality over quantity — divesting underperforming brands and focusing investment on the strongest names.

Recent Catalysts

Gucci creative reset: The appointment of a new creative director to replace Alessandro Michele has been underway. The market is waiting to see whether the new creative direction can restore Gucci's desirability and stop the market-share losses to Louis Vuitton. Luxury sector headwinds: Post-pandemic luxury spending has normalised. Chinese consumer confidence remains weak, affecting the high-spending tourist and aspirational luxury buyer. This has broadly impacted the luxury sector — Kering is not alone. Saint Laurent and Bottega Veneta: These brands have been more stable than Gucci and provide some earnings diversification. Bottega's intrecciato leather goods have built genuine brand equity; Saint Laurent remains a solid mid-tier luxury brand. Balance sheet and capital return: Kering has been divesting non-core assets and returning capital. The balance sheet remains a strength relative to some peers.

Key Risks

  • 11 Gucci brand reset risk — This is the dominant single-company risk. If the new creative director fails to restore Gucci's desirability, the group's earnings and multiple will remain under pressure. Gucci accounts for ~50% of group revenue.
  • 22 China and aspirational luxury weakness — Chinese consumers account for a significant share of luxury spending globally. Consumer confidence in China remains soft, and the aspirational luxury buyer — who trades up to Gucci from mid-market — has become more cautious.
  • 33 Luxury sector multiple compression — The luxury sector has re-rated down from 2021–2022 peaks as growth normalised. If growth continues to disappoint, sector multiples compress further, dragging Kering with them.
  • 44 Competition from LVMH — Louis Vuitton has been more resilient than Gucci through the sector's recent softness. LVMH's diversified brand portfolio and strong execution has taken share. If this persists, Kering's competitive position deteriorates structurally.
