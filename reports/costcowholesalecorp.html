Business Model

Costco operates on a high-volume, low-margin membership warehouse model that creates a near-unbreakable competitive moat:

  • Membership Fees: The core economic engine. Costco charges an annual membership fee (Gold Star $65, Business $65, Executive $130) that is nearly pure profit. With ~76 million paid members and a renewal rate exceeding 93 per cent, this is one of the most predictable revenue streams in retail.
  • Warehouse Sales: A curated selection of approximately 4,000 SKUs across groceries, electronics, appliances, apparel, tyres, and home goods. Costco sells at thin gross margins (approximately 11-12 per cent net of membership benefit) but extremely high inventory turnover.
  • E-commerce and ancillary services: Costco.com for home delivery, Costco Travel, optical centres, hearing aid centres, pharmacies, and food courts - all serving to deepen member engagement and increase visit frequency.
  • Kirkland Signature: The proprietary Kirkland Signature brand delivers higher-margin private-label products across food, apparel, and household goods - increasingly important to margin stability.

Costco's genius is using ultra-low prices to attract members, then generating nearly all its operating profit from membership fees while the merchandise sales cover costs. It is one of the most capital-efficient retail models ever constructed.

50 / 100 A consumer spending slowdown disproportionately hits warehouse retailers as middle-income members trade down to discount formats. Sam's Club and BJ's increase competitive pressure. Key Risks

1Valuation stretch: At 52x earnings, the stock prices in near-perfect execution. Any meaningful deceleration in comparable sales or membership growth would likely trigger a significant de-rating given the premium multiples involved.
2Consumer spending deterioration: Costco's members, while relatively affluent, are not immune to a broad economic slowdown. A severe recession could reduce basket size, visit frequency, and ultimately renewal rates - particularly as $65-130 annual memberships become easier to cancel.
3Competitive pressure from discount formats: Dollar Tree, Aldi, and regional discount grocers continue expanding, and if consumers trade down permanently from warehouse to discount grocery, Costco's comp trajectory would be structurally impaired.
4Labour cost inflation: Warehouse retail is labour-intensive. Continued minimum wage increases and union pressure (Costco workers are among the best-paid in the sector) could compress merchandise margins over time.
5International execution risk: Rapid international expansion - particularly in Asia where supply chains, real estate, and consumer preferences are more complex - creates execution risk that could impair new market productivity and return on invested capital.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Appropriate For

  • Long-term compounders who want best-in-class retail quality with defensible competitive positioning and a management team that has demonstrated consistent execution over decades
  • Investors willing to pay a premium for near-perfect membership economics and the optionality of international expansion
  • Those building a generational portfolio with a 5-10 year horizon who can ride out valuation volatility in exchange for structural franchise value appreciation

Avoid If

  • Focused on valuation or near-term total return; the P/E of 52x leaves almost no room for error and limited upside to fair value over a 1-2 year horizon
  • Portfolio already holds other high-quality compounding retail names (e.g., Walmart, Home Depot) - Costco adds less incremental value at this price
  • Strong macro concerns about a US consumer slowdown that would disproportionately impact warehouse-format retailers
