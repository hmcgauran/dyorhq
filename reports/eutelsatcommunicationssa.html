Executive Summary

Eutelsat has been absolutely destroyed - down ~51% from its 52-week high of EUR 4.74 to EUR 2.33. The stock is sitting 47% above its 52-week low of EUR 1.59, having recovered from the lows but still in a severe downtrend. This is not a value opportunity in the traditional sense - it is a business under structural pressure from multiple directions.

The March 2026 contract terminations for Express AT1 and AT2 (Russian satellite capacity, sanctions-related) are a fresh headwind. The EUR 1.5 billion bond refinancing in March addressed near-term debt maturity but does not solve the underlying revenue pressure. The OneWeb LEO constellation is the only genuinely interesting catalyst - if it works.

Bottom line: REDUCE. The stock has been crushed and there are legitimate bulls who argue the debt restructuring is priced in and OneWeb is the real story. They may be right. But the track record of LEO satellite constellations delivering on connectivity promises is mixed (see: SoftBank's WeWork-like cycle of overpromising), and Eutelsat's balance sheet has limited room for error. Wait for clarity on OneWeb commercial traction before treating this as a value play.

Business Model

Eutelsat is a satellite operator - it owns and operates a fleet of geostationary communications satellites providing video, data, and internet connectivity services to broadcast customers, telecom operators, and enterprise clients globally.

Revenue streams:

  • Video & Broadcast (~40%): Satellite capacity for TV broadcasting - the legacy cash cow. Under pressure as viewers migrate to streaming.
  • Government & Enterprise (~35%): Secure communications, military/government contracts, maritime and aviation connectivity.
  • Mobile Connectivity (~15%): Backhaul for mobile networks, aviation and maritime connectivity.
  • OneWeb LEO (~10% and growing): Low Earth Orbit constellation for broadband - the strategic bet. Operational but pre-commercial scale.

Economic moat: orbital slots are scarce resources - once Eutelsat holds a geostationary slot, competitors cannot easily replicate it. Long-term contracts with broadcast clients provide revenue visibility. The moat is eroding as LEO constellations (Starlink, OneWeb) offer lower-latency alternatives.

Key Risks

  1. Debt burden and refinancing risk (HIGH): The EUR 1.5B refinancing addressed near-term maturities but the debt load remains enormous for a declining-revenue business. If EBITDA erodes faster than modelled, covenant breaches become a real possibility. This is the primary equity risk.
  2. Revenue decline acceleration (HIGH): Video broadcast is structural decline, not cyclical. Every year, more viewers leave satellite for streaming. Eutelsat's addressable market is shrinking. The pace of decline will determine whether the debt trajectory is manageable.
  3. OneWeb execution risk (MEDIUM-HIGH): OneWeb is the only meaningful growth lever. It is capital-intensive, pre-revenue at scale, and faces direct competition from Starlink. French export credit financing is helpful but does not guarantee commercial success.
  4. Geopolitical risk (MEDIUM): The Express AT1/AT2 terminations (Russian sanctions) illustrate this. Eutelsat has exposure to markets with geopolitical tension. European government contracts partially offset this.
  5. LEO constellation competition (MEDIUM): Starlink has dramatically more satellites deployed and a functioning commercial product. OneWeb is behind and needs to scale fast to be relevant in connectivity.
  6. Liquidity / equity raise risk (MEDIUM): A EUR 1.1B market cap and EUR 1.5B+ debt means the equity is thin. If the business needs more capital, an equity raise at these levels would be massively dilutive to existing holders.
