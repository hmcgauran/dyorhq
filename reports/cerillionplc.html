Executive Summary

Cerillion PLC is a London-listed specialist in billing, revenue management, and customer experience software for telecoms and utilities. Its pre-paid and post-paid billing platforms, CRM layer, and data analytics suite serve mobile operators, broadband providers, and utility companies across the UK, Europe, and the US. The business operates on a SaaS model with annuity-style recurring revenue, giving it defensible visibility against the more lumpy capital expenditure cycles of its telco client base.

At 1440.00 GBX, the stock trades on a P/E of approximately 25x against an estimated EPS of 57p, implying a market capitalisation of roughly GBP150 million. That is not demanding for a software company with recurring revenue characteristics and exposure to secular growth themes in telecom network monetisation and utility billing digitisation. The key question is whether the growth rate justifies the multiple and whether the small-cap register can absorb institutional interest as the story matures.

Bottom line: BUY. Cerillion occupies a defensible niche in BSS (Business Support Systems) software. The SaaS transition is underway and should improve revenue quality over time. On a sum-of-parts view, the billing platform and analytics business are worth more than the current market cap implies. Add on weakness related to short-term contract timing.

Business Model

Cerillion provides an integrated BSS platform covering pre-paid and post-paid billing, rating, charging, CRM, and data analytics for telecoms and utilities. The software sits at the intersection of network infrastructure and customer-facing operations: it handles everything from rating voice and data packages to managing billing cycles, customer churn signals, and usage analytics. Customers include mobile virtual network operators (MVNOs), fixed-line broadband providers, and utility companies running smart meter or digital billing programmes.

The business has been transitioning from on-premise licensing to a cloud/SaaS delivery model, which improves revenue visibility and reduces client deployment complexity. Recurring maintenance and support fees form the backbone of annuity revenue, supplemented by implementation and professional services income tied to new customer onboarding.

The competitive position is narrow but durable: BSS software requires deep integration with client billing logic and regulatory requirements, creating meaningful switching costs for established customers. Cerillion's telco-specific expertise is a barrier to entry against generic ERP or CRM vendors, while the scale required to build a credible global BSS platform protects against smaller challengers. The primary risk is customer concentration: a small number of telco clients can represent a large proportion of revenue, making individual contract wins and losses structurally significant.

Recent Catalysts

  • SaaS transition momentum: Cerillion has been actively migrating its on-premise client base to cloud delivery. This converts one-off licence revenue to recurring subscriptions, improving revenue quality and net revenue retention. Each successful migration is a direct margin benefit.
  • Utility sector expansion: Smart meter rollout and utility billing digitisation programmes in the UK and Europe represent a growing addressable market for Cerillion's billing and CRM capabilities. Utility clients typically have longer contract durations and higher switching costs than telecoms, making them attractive adjacencies.
  • US market traction: The US telco market is structurally significant given its scale. Any meaningful new customer wins among US regional operators or cable companies would be a material catalyst for revenue growth and investor confidence in the international expansion thesis.
  • AIM listing liquidity: As an AIM-listed small cap, Cerillion benefits from the potential for increased institutional coverage and index inclusion eligibility as the business scales. The London listing also provides a credible platform for M&A consideration from larger BSS players evaluating bolt-on acquisitions.

Key Risks

  1. Customer concentration (HIGH): As a small-cap BSS vendor, Cerillion is likely to have meaningful revenue concentration among a small number of telecoms clients. The loss of a major telco account would have a disproportionate P&L impact. This is the single most important risk to monitor in any investor presentation.
  2. Telco capex cyclicality (MEDIUM): Telecoms operators run multi-year network investment cycles. When capex is directed toward infrastructure (5G rollout, fibre build-out), BSS spending can be deferred. Cerillion's revenue is partially exposed to this cyclicality via new implementation revenue, even if recurring maintenance is more stable.
  3. SaaS transition execution risk (MEDIUM): The migration from on-premise to cloud is the right strategic direction, but execution risk is real. Customer migration projects can slip, consume professional services resources, and temporarily suppress margins. The pace of transition directly affects the revenue quality investors are pricing the stock on.
  4. Small-cap liquidity and register (MEDIUM): AIM-listed micro-caps with market caps below GBP200m typically lack sell-side coverage and have limited institutional register. This creates both a valuation discount and a liquidity risk for investors who need to size in or out of positions efficiently.
