1. Executive Summary

General Motors is in the early stages of an EV transition that could redefine its long-term competitive position, but legacy ICE margins, unionised labour costs, and Chinese joint venture uncertainty make the near-term outlook uncertain at current levels.

2. Business Model

General Motors designs, manufactures, and sells vehicles across four segments: (1) North America (Chevrolet, GMC, Cadillac, Buick retail and fleet); (2) International (China joint ventures with SAIC-GM and FAW-GM, plus exports to emerging markets); (3) Cruise (autonomous vehicle development, currently paused for regulatory review); and (4) Financial Services (GM Financial auto lending, insurance, and fleet management). Revenue is dominated by truck and SUV sales in North America, which generate ~65% of EBIT through US unionised manufacturing. The EV portfolio (Chevy Blazer EV, Silverado EV, BrightDrop commercial vans) is growing but currently loss-making on an EBIT basis as the company scales battery production and navigates the learning curve.

GM is a classic cyclical value trap — the P/E of 23.4x looks reasonable until you adjust for the EV loss burden and unusual items. The underlying North America truck business generates excellent returns (wholesale ASPs above $60,000 on full-size trucks, EBIT margins approaching 15%). The bear case centres on EV losses (~$4-5bn annually through FY2026) consuming ICE cash generation, and the Chinese market JV contributing less as local brands (BYD, Li Auto) capture share. The Cruise AV pause removes a speculative premium but also eliminates a long-term optionality leg.

7. Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Ideal for: Contrarian industrial investors; those who believe EV transition risk is priced in at current levels; option-value seekers on Cruise AV restart.

Avoid if: You require earnings visibility (EV losses make forward P/E murky); you want clean automotive exposure without legacy ICE and union drag; you are concerned about China market share.
