Business Model

Diageo plc is a global alcoholic beverage company operating across five geographic segments: North America, Europe, Africa, Latin America and Caribbean, and Asia Pacific. The group's portfolio spans spirits, beer, and ready-to-drink products, with leading brands including Johnnie Walker (whisky), Guinness (stout), Tanqueray (gin), Baileys (cream liqueur), Captain Morgan (rum), and Smirnoff (vodka). Diageo's model is premised on premiumisation -- the idea that consumers trade up to higher-margin super-premium and prestige brands over time. The group manufactures and distributes via a combination of owned production facilities and third-party contract arrangements. Revenue is generated through sales to wholesale distributors (primarily North America and Europe) and directly to retail customers in some markets. The business is highly cash generative, with a disciplined approach to capital allocation including a progressive dividend policy and regular share buybacks.

Key Risks

  1. #1 New CEO execution risk: Until Diageo's new CEO articulates a clear strategic roadmap, investors face a leadership vacuum premium that keeps the stock range-bound and risks further de-rating if the strategy disappoints.
  2. #2 North American channel inventory: Distributor destocking in North America has suppressed top-line growth. The pace and completeness of inventory normalisation is the most important near-term swing factor for earnings.
  3. #3 Premiumisation model stress test: Diageo's growth model depends on consumers trading up to super-premium and prestige brands. A structural shift in consumer behaviour -- economic pressure, health consciousness, moderation trends -- would impair the core thesis.
  4. #4 Emerging market exposure: Diageo has significant Africa and Asia Pacific exposure. Currency depreciation, geopolitical instability, regulatory changes, or economic downturns in these markets could significantly impact earnings.
  5. #5 Input cost and margin pressure: Grain, glass, and logistics inflation can compress margins. Diageo has some pricing power, but there is a lag between input cost increases and pricing realisation that can create short-term margin squeeze.
