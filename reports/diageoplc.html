Executive Summary

Diageo is a quality compounder trading at decade-low valuations following a significant operational reset. The February 2026 H1 results delivered a kitchen-sink moment: dividend halved, organic guidance cut to -2% to -3%, and a frank admission that volume recovery in the US and Asia will take longer than previously guided.

Incoming CEO Sir Dave Lewis brings a credible turnaround template from his Tesco tenure, and the $2.3bn East Africa disposal at 17x EBITDA demonstrates management's willingness to crystallise value. However, with 35% of revenue exposed to a structurally pressured US spirits market and Chinese baijiu dragging reported numbers, the risk/reward does not yet justify a BUY.

Bottom line: HOLD. Add below 1,300p or on confirmed US volume recovery. August 2026 full-year results are the key re-rating catalyst.

Business Model

Diageo is the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue. Its portfolio spans Scotch whisky (Johnnie Walker - the world's best-selling Scotch), Irish stout (Guinness), gin (Tanqueray, Gordon's), Irish cream (Baileys), and tequila (Casamigos, Don Julio). The group has ~200 brands sold in ~180 markets.

Revenue is approximately 60% Americas and Europe, 40% Asia, Africa and emerging markets. The core strategy is premiumisation - pushing volume up the price ladder across whisky, tequila, and gin categories - supplemented by portfolio rationalisation via disposals of non-core or lower-margin assets.

The economic moat is genuine: brand equity accumulated over decades, distribution locked in through on-trade relationships globally, and barriers to replicating aged spirit stocks at scale. The vulnerability is cyclicality - premium spirits are a discretionary purchase sensitive to consumer confidence, particularly in the key US market.

Recent Catalysts

  • H1 FY26 results (25 Feb 2026): Kitchen-sink reset. Organic net sales guidance cut to -2% to -3% for the full year. Dividend halved. Management explicitly acknowledged the US spirits category is recovering more slowly than forecast.
  • New CEO - Sir Dave Lewis (in post 1 Jan 2026): Former CEO of Tesco, where he executed a successful multi-year turnaround. Strong track record of portfolio rationalisation, cost discipline, and credibility restoration with institutional shareholders.
  • US spirits category weakness: The US represents ~35% of revenue. Post-pandemic normalisation has proven deeper and more persistent than Diageo or peers anticipated, with category volumes declining across premium spirits.
  • Chinese baijiu decline: Diageo's Shui Jing Fang brand and broader exposure to Chinese white spirits has been a drag on Asia numbers. Consumer confidence in China remains soft, and the baijiu premium category has seen meaningful volume declines.
  • $2.3bn East Africa disposal (H2 2026): Sale of the East Africa business at 17x EBITDA - above market expectations. Proceeds will partially delever the balance sheet and demonstrate management's commitment to portfolio optimisation.

Key Risks

  1. 1 US spirits structural decline - If the post-pandemic premium spirits volume decline is structural (secular shift toward beer, RTDs, or simply reduced alcohol consumption) rather than cyclical, the recovery thesis fails. 35% of revenue exposure makes this the single largest risk.
  2. 2 China baijiu drag - Chinese consumer confidence recovery is uncertain. Shui Jing Fang and broader baijiu exposure could continue to drag reported numbers and management attention.
  3. 3 Net debt $13bn - Limits M&A optionality and share buybacks. If FCF deteriorates below the $3bn run-rate, leverage metrics will deteriorate and credit rating risk emerges.
  4. 4 Dividend cut effect - Income investors who owned Diageo for its reliable, growing dividend have been structurally de-motivated. The income floor is gone; re-building yield credibility takes multiple years of execution.
  5. 5 Lewis turnaround pace - Tesco was a domestic UK business; Diageo is a 200-brand global operation across 180 markets. The turnaround may be slower and more complex than the Tesco precedent suggests, testing investor patience.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Long-term value investors seeking quality defensive exposure at decade-low valuations. Those who believe in the premium spirits category over a 3-5 year horizon and trust the new CEO's turnaround track record. Investors comfortable sitting through a slow-burning recovery without near-term catalysts.

Should avoid: Anyone needing near-term price catalysts, income from a growing dividend, or who bought Diageo for its previous reputation as a reliable dividend compounder. Short-term traders and momentum investors should also stay away - the stock is structurally out of favour and the re-rating requires genuine operational improvement, not narrative.
