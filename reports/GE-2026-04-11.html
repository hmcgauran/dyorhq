Executive Summary

General Electric has executed a remarkable operational turnaround under CEO Larry Culp, with the aviation franchise proving exceptional, but the shares have re-rated dramatically from their 2020 lows and now trade at a P/E of 37.7× that prices in considerable execution success — a Reduce is warranted at this level.

Business Model

General Electric is an industrial conglomerate focused on three core businesses following years of portfolio simplification:

  • GE Aerospace: The crown jewel. Aircraft engines (LEAP, GE9X) and aviation systems for commercial and military aircraft. The LEAP engine (co-produced with Safran) powers the Airbus A320neo family and is in high-demand given the aircraft backlog. GE9X powers the Boeing 777X. This segment generates the highest margins and most durable growth in the portfolio.
  • GE Vernova: Energy infrastructure including gas turbines, steam turbines, power conversion, and grid solutions. The business is a beneficiary of AI data-centre power demand and grid modernisation spending globally. GE Vernova is a separate publicly traded entity following its 2024 spinoff.
  • GE HealthCare: Medical imaging equipment (MRI, CT, X-ray), ultrasound, and digital infrastructure for healthcare. Spun off in January 2023 as a separately traded company. GE retains a meaningful stake.

The investment case centres on the GE Aerospace franchise — one of the few truly dominant industrial franchises globally, with a large installed base generating recurring aftermarket revenue from spare parts, maintenance, and upgrades.

Recent Catalysts (Last 3–6 Months)

  • Aviation demand surge: Commercial air travel demand has recovered strongly, with airline profitability driving fleet expansion and engine replacement cycles. Boeing delivery delays have paradoxically benefited GE as airlines keep older aircraft flying longer, extending engine overhaul cycles.
  • LEAP engine production ramp: LEAP engine production has reached full production rate, with increasing aftermarket revenue as the installed base matures into the maintenance cycle. This creates a high-margin recurring revenue tailwind for years to come.
  • GE Vernova spinoff: The successful spinoff of GE Vernova in April 2024 created two focused industrial companies. GE Aerospace is now a pure-play aviation business; GE Vernova's own valuation has validated the strategic rationale.
  • GE HealthCare stake reduction: GE has been reducing its stake in GE HealthCare, crystallising value for shareholders while maintaining strategic optionality through retained minority positions.
  • Supply chain recovery: GE Aerospace's supply chain has recovered from COVID-era disruptions, allowing the business to fulfil orders without the premium spot-market sourcing costs that compressed margins in prior years.

Key Risks

1GE Aerospace multiple concentration: Following the spinoffs, GE's entire investment case rests on the aviation business. Any meaningful deterioration in the commercial aviation cycle, LEAP pricing pressure, or competitive encroachment (Rolls-Royce Trent) would have an outsized impact on the valuation.
2Boeing dependency: GE Aerospace is the exclusive engine supplier for the Boeing 737 MAX and 777X. Boeing's well-documented quality and production challenges could slow engine offtake and impair the revenue trajectory for these programmes.
3LEAP programme risks: The LEAP engine is a critical revenue driver. Geopolitical trade disruptions (particularly between the US and France/Safran joint venture partners), supply chain constraints, or quality issues with the LEAP could impair the most important programme in the portfolio.
4SAF and hydrogen transition costs: The aviation industry faces regulatory pressure to adopt sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) and hydrogen-compatible engines. The R&D and capital expenditure required to develop these next-generation technologies could be substantial without clear cost recovery timelines.
5Aftermarket margin normalisation: The high-margin aftermarket business depends on a large and ageing installed base. As newer engines (LEAP) enter the fleet, the mix shift from mature high-margin overhauls to newer engine service contracts could pressure segment margins.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Appropriate For

  • Long-term industrial investors who want the premier aviation franchise globally and are willing to accept a premium valuation in exchange for a business with a secular growth tailwind and a durable aftermarket revenue moat
  • Thematic investors in the global travel recovery and fleet expansion cycle who want broad aviation exposure without single-airline or single-platform risk
  • Those who believe Culp-era operational improvements are still early in their realisation, with significant margin expansion still to come from the aerospace business

Avoid If

  • Seeking value or a reasonable entry P/E — at 37.7× earnings the shares price in very high-quality execution with almost no margin of safety for unexpected setbacks
  • Concerned about Boeing's production recovery trajectory or the broader commercial aviation cycle's sensitivity to macroeconomic conditions
  • Portfolio already holds Rolls-Royce (RYCEY) or Honeywell (HON) which provide overlapping aerospace exposure at more reasonable valuations
