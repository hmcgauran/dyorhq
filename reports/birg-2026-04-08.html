Executive Summary

Bank of Ireland is the oldest bank in Ireland and the second-largest by market share in the Republic of Ireland, operating a retail banking franchise, corporate and SME lending, wealth management, and General Insurance distribution. At 15.72p the stock is recovering from its 52-week low of 9.07p — having traded as low as 9p in late 2025 — but remains materially below its 52-week high of 18.02p. The recovery reflects improving sentiment toward Irish banking following AIB's dividend restart and the sector's improved capital generation.

The investment case centres on normalised net interest income in a higher-rate environment, improving capital return capacity, and the operational leverage available from cost-saving programmes. The risks are a slowing Irish economy, mortgage competition, and the perennial political risk around tracker mortgage remediation costs.

Bottom line: HOLD. The reset from tracker mortgage provisions and the improving capital return story make this a reasonable hold, but the near-term upside from current levels is limited without a clear catalyst.

Business Model

Bank of Ireland operates across four divisions: Retail Ireland (mortgages, personal current accounts, deposits), Corporate and Commercial (SME and large corporate lending), Wealth and Insurance (life and general insurance distribution, wealth management), and UK operations. The core business is Irish mortgage and SME lending funded by customer deposits.

The bank's structural advantage is its distribution franchise — 250+ branches and a deep relationship with Irish consumers and SMEs. The competitive landscape has improved following the Permanent TSB rescue: AIB and BOIG are effectively the two dominant players in a market that is structurally more concentrated than the UK or European markets.

The NIM (net interest margin) benefit from higher ECB rates has been significant — Irish banks with tracker mortgage books benefited disproportionately from the rapid rate rise cycle. As rates normalise, NIM will compress, which is the central earnings headwind for the sector.

Recent Catalysts

  • Tracker mortgage remediation conclusion: The tracker mortgage investigation — which imposed significant conduct charges and customer remediation costs — is largely concluded. This removes a major uncertainty overhang from the bank's regulatory risk profile.
  • ECB rate normalisation: As the ECB cuts rates, the NIM compression for Irish banks with large tracker books will accelerate. This is a headwind, not a catalyst, but the market appears to be pricing in the worst of it.
  • Capital return capacity: BOIG's CET1 ratio provides meaningful capacity for dividends and buybacks. The market is ожидание (expecting) progressive capital return as the tracker storm passes.
  • Irish economy: Ireland's GDP figures remain distorted by multinational restructuring, but the domestic economy is slowing. Mortgage demand remains reasonable but credit quality deterioration is a risk if unemployment rises.

Key Risks

  1. 1NIM compression: As ECB rates fall, the tracker mortgage book will cause NIM to compress faster than for most European banks. Irish banks with large tracker books are the most exposed to rate cuts — the opposite of the tailwind they enjoyed during the rate rise cycle.
  2. 2Irish credit quality: Irish mortgage arrears and SME default rates are at cyclical lows. Any economic softening — driven by housing market correction or unemployment rise — would pressure the loan loss provision charge and reduce earnings.
  3. 3Tracker regulatory risk: While the main remediation is concluded, the Central Bank of Ireland has demonstrated willingness to pursue conduct issues at scale. Further regulatory risk is possible.
  4. 4Competitive pressure on mortgages: EBS (AIB), Avantcard, and non-bank lenders are competing aggressively for Irish mortgage share. This limits repricing ability and puts pressure on the core franchise.

Who Should Own It / Avoid It

Should own: Investors wanting conventional financial sector exposure with a domestic Irish economic angle. Those who believe the tracker storm is behind the bank and capital return will accelerate. Income investors who want a progressive dividend from a bank that has exited the regulatory penalty box.

Should avoid: Investors looking for growth — this is a mature, domestic banking franchise with limited upside beyond capital return and dividend yield. Anyone with strong views on ECB rate direction should note the inverse relationship between ECB cuts and Irish bank NIMs.
