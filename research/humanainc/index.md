## Business Model
Humana is one of the largest US Medicare Advantage (MA) managed care organisations, with approximately 5.3 million medical plan members as of FY2025. The core business model involves contracting with the US federal government (Centres for Medicare and Medicaid Services, CMS) to provide health benefits to Medicare-eligible seniors under MA plans - these are Medicare Advantage Prescription Drug (MAPD) and standalone Prescription Drug Plan (PDP) products. Premium revenues are received from CMS (risk-adjusted, based on member demographics and health status) and from member premiums. The MA model is capitated: Humana receives a fixed per-member per-month (PMPM) premium from the government, bears the medical cost risk, and earns the difference between premium revenues and medical costs incurred. This creates strong incentives for care management, chronic disease management, and utilisation control - Humana's CarePoint (proprietary provider engagement platform) and clinical programmes are designed to manage this risk while improving member outcomes. The key revenue metrics are MBR (medical benefit ratio, the inverse of margin), Stars ratings (CMS quality bonus programme), and membership growth. Higher Stars ratings (4+ stars) unlock quality bonus payments that are material to the economics - at 4-star level, a health plan receives a 5% quality bonus on base premium; at 5-star level it can receive 5% plus a 3% quality bonus. Approximately 66% of Humana's members were in 4+ star plans as of the last reporting period, but Stars maintenance is increasingly difficult as CMS raises the bar. Humana also operates a commercial segment (employer group plans) and a military/Veterans health contract (TRICARE), but Medicare is the dominant business by membership and earnings.

## Key Risks
1. Medicare Advantage reimbursement and Stars ratings risk: This is the dominant near-term risk. CMS sets MA reimbursement rates annually via the Advance Notice and Final Rate Notice. Any net negative rate change (or higher-than-expected benefit cut) would directly impair Humana's earnings. Similarly, any Stars downgrade directly reduces quality bonus income, which can be worth $2-3 per share in earnings. The dual risk of rate pressure and Stars downgrades simultaneously is the worst-case scenario for MA investors.
2. Medical cost ratio (MCR) inflation: Post-COVID utilisation has remained elevated. Humana's MBR (inverse of margin) has deteriorated, and if it continues, earnings will miss guidance. The challenge is that seniors are deferring less care now that the pandemic is over, and chronic disease burden is growing - this is a structural headwind for the entire MA industry, not just Humana.
3. Competitive dynamics: UnitedHealth Group has been aggressively growing its MA membership, leveraging its scale and integrated care model (Optum). Regional players are also competing hard, and CVS/Aetna (through its Medicare business) is a significant competitor. This competitive intensity makes Stars maintenance harder and pricing discipline more difficult to maintain.
4. Political and regulatory risk: Any federal action to cut MA reimbursement rates, impose a maximum cost model, or increase regulatory burden on MA plans would be materially negative for the sector. This risk has increased under the Trump administration's cost-cutting posture.
5. Debt levels and capital return: Humana has been investing heavily in care platform infrastructure andStars improvement programmes while simultaneously navigating a challenging MBR environment. If MBR does not improve as management expects, the company may face a cash flow squeeze that constrains capital return. The current market cap of $23 billion reflects a company with limited financial flexibility.
