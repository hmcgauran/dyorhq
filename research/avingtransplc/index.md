## Business Model
Avingtrans operates through a small portfolio of specialist engineering subsidiaries, each serving distinct but related end markets. The group's capabilities are anchored in precision fabrication and machining: heat exchangers for energy applications, precision components for aerospace platforms, and bespoke fabricated solutions for industrial customers. This is a business built on technical accreditation, long supplier qualification cycles, and sticky customer relationships rather than commodity volume. Revenue is predominantly contract-based with a mix of long-term framework agreements and project-specific orders. Margins in precision engineering are structurally higher than general fabrication due to qualification barriers, but Avingtrans operates in a competitive landscape that includes larger players with greater scale advantages in procurement and capacity utilisation. The key operational metric is capacity utilisation across the manufacturing footprint: the business is most profitable when it is running hot on aerospace and energy programmes. The competitive moat is accreditational: aerospace and nuclear-adjacent customers require lengthy supplier qualification. Once qualified, Avingtrans tends to retain positions on programme. This creates a durable, if unglamorous, revenue base.
