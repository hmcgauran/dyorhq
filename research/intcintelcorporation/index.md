## Business Model
Intel designs and manufactures CPUs, GPUs, FPGAs, and network chips. Its business historically comprised three segments: Client Computing (PC and laptop CPUs), Data Centre and AI (server CPUs, Gaudi AI accelerators), and Network and Edge. Intel Foundry Services (IFS) represents the company's ambition to become a third-party chip manufacturer, competing with TSMC - an expensive and historically loss-making undertaking. The companyfabless-in-design but foundry-in-ambition structure creates a fundamental tension: the foundry investment requires massive capital expenditure that depresses near-term earnings, while the competitive position of the core CPU business deteriorates during the investment period. Intel is essentially attempting two difficult transitions simultaneously - losing a CPU cycle and building a foundry - while its cash generation is challenged. EPS is negative on a reported basis (GAAP), with the P/E showing as N/A due to the net loss. This makes valuation comparison difficult and implies the market is pricing the stock on a sum-of-parts or restructuring basis, not earnings multiples.

## Recent Catalysts
CHIPS Act funding: Intel received ~$8.5 billion in direct CHIPS Act grants and up to $11 billion in loans, which partially funds its US manufacturing expansion. This is a genuine subsidy but must be weighed against the much larger capital Intel is self-funding. Gaudi 3 launch: Intel's Gaudi 3 AI accelerator was positioned as a cost-effective alternative to NVIDIA's H100. Adoption has been slower than hoped, and hyperscalers have largely committed to NVIDIA and AMD for their AI compute buildout. Xeon 6 and Core Ultra: Intel's latest CPU generations have been reasonably competitive on performance-per-watt but have not reversed AMD EPYC market share gains. The PC CPU recovery has been muted. Foundry losses: Intel Foundry Services continues to report operating losses. The path to breakeven requires achieving meaningful customer scale - currently Intel is its own best customer, which limits the credibility of third-party use cases. Strategic review and potential restructuring: Reports of a potential split or restructuring of the foundry business have circulated. Any separation could unlock value but also reveals the depth of the operational challenge.

## Key Risks
1. Capital destruction in foundry: The IFS capex cycle is long and the competitive landscape (TSMC, Samsung) is entrenched. Intel may spend tens of billions and still not achieve competitive yields at leading edge - the bear scenario has meaningful probability.
2. AI accelerator competitive gap: Gaudi is at best third-choice after NVIDIA and AMD for AI accelerator. The CUDA moat is real, and Gaudi's software ecosystem is far behind ROCm, let alone CUDA. Winning meaningful AI workloads is very difficult.
3. CPU competitive erosion: AMD EPYC has taken substantial server market share and this trend has not reversed. ARM-based CPUs (AWS Graviton, Ampere, Qualcomm) represent a longer-term architecture threat that Intel has not answered.
4. Dividend sustainability: At current cash burn rates and capital expenditure, the 2.5%+ dividend yield may not be sustainable. A dividend cut would be a significant negative catalyst given the stock's appeal to yield-seeking buyers.
5. Geopolitical and manufacturing concentration: Intel's US and EU manufacturing investments are politically motivated as much as commercially motivated. If CHIPS Act subsidies are reduced or trade policy shifts, the economics of the foundry investment deteriorate further.
