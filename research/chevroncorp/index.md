## Executive Summary

## Business Model
Chevron is the second-largest US oil major, with operations spanning upstream (exploration and production) and downstream (refining and marketing). Key upstream assets include the Permian Basin (the most prolific US shale play, where Chevron is a top-two operator following the PDC Energy acquisition), LNG export facilities, deepwater Gulf of Mexico assets, and international positions in Australia (LNG), Kazakhstan and the DJ Basin (Colorado). The downstream segment includes Chevron's El Segundo and Richmond (California) refineries, plus a global marketing and retail network. The Hess Corporation acquisition ($53 billion, announced 2023) - if it closes - would add meaningful Bakken shale exposure and a stake in the Guyana Stabroek block (one of the world's most prolific new oil provinces). The tie-up with BrightCycle (renewable fuels) and Geothermal development represents modest, early-stage energy transition positioning. Cash generation is strong and the dividend (yield ~4.1%) is well-supported by free cash flow at $50-60 oil.

Chevron's Permian Basin production continues to grow, with the PDC Energy acquisition adding ~100,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day of production. The Hess acquisition remains in regulatory review - the key issue is whether the US FTC approves the deal and resolves the concurrent right-of-first-refusal dispute with Exxon Mobil over the Guyana Stabroek block. Chevron's 2025 full-year production guidance pointed to ~3.5 million barrels of oil equivalent per day. The buyback programme ($15-17 billion annually) has been sustained, providing capital return even in a lower oil price environment.

## Key Risks
1. Oil price cyclicality: At $75-85 oil, Chevron generates strong free cash flow. Below $65, the dividend and buyback programme come under pressure, and the investment case materially weakens. Oil price forecasting is notoriously unreliable, and geopolitical supply disruptions (which can spike prices) are typically short-lived.
2. Hess acquisition execution and regulatory risk: The $53 billion Hess acquisition is the most important near-term catalyst. Regulatory approval from the FTC is not guaranteed, and the concurrent ExxonMobil right-of-first-refusal dispute over Guyana adds legal complexity. If the deal fails, Chevron loses a major production accretion opportunity and the $53 billion premium paid.
3. Energy transition policy risk: Biden-era energy policies (including methane fees and leasing restrictions) have created regulatory uncertainty for US oil and gas producers. A future Democratic administration could reimpose permitting delays and increase regulatory costs.
4. Permian Basin concentration and divestiture risk: The Permian is becoming increasingly central to Chevron's portfolio. Any operational disruption, regulatory constraint or infrastructure bottleneck (pipeline capacity, water disposal) in the Permian would disproportionately affect group earnings.
5. International asset base complexity: Chevron's international assets (Kazakhstan, Australia LNG, deepwater Gulf) carry geopolitical risk (Russia-Ukraine spillover, South China Sea tensions) and operational complexity (long-cycle projects with fixed cost structures).
