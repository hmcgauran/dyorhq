## Business Model
Phillips 66 is a major US downstream energy company spun off from ConocoPhillips in 2012. The business is organised into three segments: R refining and Marketing (R&M): The core business, operating 13 refineries across the US with total crude processing capacity of approximately 2.9 million barrels per day. The refinery system is geographically advantaged, with a high proportion of coastal assets (including the 330,000 bpd Los Angeles refinery and the 150,000 bpd San Francisco-area refinery) that benefit from constrained West Coast gasoline crack spreads. The R&M segment transforms crude oil into refined products - petrol, diesel, jet fuel, and petrochemicals - and sells these through Phillips 66, 76, and Conoco branded retail sites (approximately 7,600 outlets across the US). Midstream (including CPChem): Phillips 66 owns 50% of Chevron Phillips Chemical (CPChem), a major olefins and polyolefins producer. This joint venture provides a higher-growth, higher-margin chemicals platform and partial diversification from pure refining. Specialty Lubricants and Other: The company produces and markets lubricants, basestocks, and waxes under brands including Phillips 66 Lubricants and Kendall. Capital allocation has been aggressive: the company has returned significant cash via dividends and buybacks, and reduced leverage following the 2023 disposal of its Western Europe refining network to bp. At current crude prices and crack spread levels, the model generates substantial free cash flow.

## Key Risks
1. Refining margin cyclicality: The 2022-2023 period was exceptional for US refiners due to supply chain disruptions and Russian refinery outages. The normalisation of crack spreads from those peaks is the most important risk to the bull thesis. Any structural increase in global refining capacity (particularly new Middle East and Asia capacity additions planned for 2025-2027) would compress margins structurally.
2. Crude oil price volatility: A sharp crude price spike (above $100) would compress refinery margins as product prices lag. A crude price collapse would be temporarily negative for refining throughput economics. The company's hedging programme mitigates but does not eliminate this risk.
3. Leverage and capital intensity: Though the company has reduced leverage following asset sales, the refining business is capital-intensive. Environmental regulatory compliance (especially in California and Washington state) requires ongoing capital expenditure on refinery upgrades. If capital expenditure requirements accelerate faster than free cash flow, buybacks could be curtailed.
4. Energy transition risk: Long-term demand destruction for petrol-powered transport would be structurally negative for petrol demand. Phillips 66's West Coast assets face the highest electrification risk in the medium term (California EV penetration rates are the highest in the US).
5. Geopolitical tariff uncertainty: Refined product trade flows are globally integrated. Any significant US tariff escalation disrupting trade flows could create crack spread volatility that is difficult to predict and position for.
