## Business Model
Jet2 operates through two principal divisions: Jet2.com (Airline): A leisure-focused low-cost carrier operating from 11 UK bases to over 70 destinations across Europe, North Africa, Turkey, the USA, and Canada. The airline is the fourth-largest in the UK by passengers carried. Its model prioritises leisure routes and short-to-medium haul operations, competing directly with easyJet and TUI Airways on sun destinations. Aircraft are predominantly Boeing 737-800 and 737-800SF variants, with the fleet modernising toward the 737 MAX 10. Jet2holidays (Package Holidays): The tour operating arm, which packages airline seats with accommodation to create ABTA-protected package holidays. This division is the primary driver of group profitability and provides a higher-margin, more predictable revenue stream than standalone airline seats. The ATOL-protected package holiday market has structural advantages: UK consumer law (the Package Travel Regulations 2018) provides strong customer protection, while ABTA membership offers dispute resolution. These protections drive demand toward organised package holidays during periods of economic uncertainty. The airline and holidays divisions are integrated: the airline provides capacity certainty for the holidays business, while the holidays business provides load factor stability for the airline. This vertical integration is the core strategic asset of the group.

## Key Risks
1. Consumer spending deterioration (HIGH): The UK household remains under pressure from elevated mortgage costs, high energy bills, and modest wage growth in real terms. Leisure travel is a category that can be deferred or downgraded - a package holiday substituted for a DIY booking, or a shorter trip in place of two weeks. The near-term demand environment is the single most important variable for Jet2's earnings.
2. Fuel cost volatility (HIGH): Jet fuel is the largest single cost component, representing approximately 25-30% of operating costs. The group hedges a majority of its fuel exposure, but unhedged positions and timing mismatches can create significant earnings volatility. A 10% sustained increase in jet fuel prices would be material to margins.
3. Competitive pricing pressure (MEDIUM): Ryanair and easyJet compete on key leisure routes. Both carriers have significantly lower cost bases than Jet2 due to lower labour costs (non-UK registered crews) and higher aircraft utilisation. Ryanair's "always low prices" strategy can force industry-wide fare compression on overlapping routes.
4. Geopolitical disruption (MEDIUM): The Western Mediterranean, Turkey, North Africa, and Eastern Europe are all exposed to geopolitical events that can redirect or suppress demand. The Turkey recovery was a tailwind; a reversal (safe-haven outflows from Turkey) would be a headwind. Greece, Jet2's largest destination, is exposed to extreme heat season impacts on tourism.
5. Regulatory and slot constraints (MEDIUM): Expansion at slot-controlled UK airports (Heathrow, Gatwick, Manchester) is constrained. Jet2's growth is weighted toward secondary airports where slot constraints are less binding, but this limits the ability to add premium trunk routes.
6. Climate transition risk (LOW-MEDIUM): The UK has committed to net-zero by 2050. SAF (Sustainable Aviation Fuel) mandates will increase operating costs for all airlines. Jet2's relatively young and modernising fleet (via the MAX 10 order) is a partial mitigation, but SAF availability and cost at scale remains a material uncertainty for the latter half of the decade.
