## Business Model
MongoDB's core product is a document database that stores data in flexible, JSON-like formats - making it particularly popular with developers building modern web, mobile, and cloud-native applications. The Community Server version is open-source; the Enterprise Server version and Atlas cloud service are commercial products. Atlas, the cloud-managed service, is where the financial model becomes compelling. It represented approximately 65% of total revenue as of FY2026 and is growing at rates materially faster than the overall business. The key metric is Atlas revenue growth rate and the percentage of Atlas customers converting to Atlas-based workloads from on-premise MongoDB deployments. The competitive moat is developer adoption - once a development team builds on MongoDB, migration costs and institutional knowledge make switching databases expensive and rare. This creates a durable installed base and pricing power in renewals.

## Recent Catalysts
Atlas AI integration: MongoDB has been adding AI and vector search capabilities to Atlas, positioning it as a data store for AI-powered applications. This is the primary near-term growth catalyst as enterprise AI adoption accelerates. Atlas consumption growth: The shift from seat-based licensing to consumption-based Atlas pricing is a structural positive - it aligns revenue with actual usage and removes seat caps that limit expansion. Enterprise demand: MongoDB's shift upmarket into Fortune 500 and government has been a meaningful growth driver. Atlas Enterprise is gaining credibility as a credible alternative to Oracle and IBM for modern application stacks. Database competition: PostgreSQL-based managed services (Supabase, Neon, AWS RDS Postgres) are growing rapidly and competing for the same workload. MongoDB's document model advantage is real but not insurmountable.
