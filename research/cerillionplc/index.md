## Business Model
Cerillion provides an integrated BSS platform covering pre-paid and post-paid billing, rating, charging, CRM, and data analytics for telecoms and utilities. The software sits at the intersection of network infrastructure and customer-facing operations: it handles everything from rating voice and data packages to managing billing cycles, customer churn signals, and usage analytics. Customers include mobile virtual network operators (MVNOs), fixed-line broadband providers, and utility companies running smart meter or digital billing programmes. The business has been transitioning from on-premise licensing to a cloud/SaaS delivery model, which improves revenue visibility and reduces client deployment complexity. Recurring maintenance and support fees form the backbone of annuity revenue, supplemented by implementation and professional services income tied to new customer onboarding. The competitive position is narrow but durable: BSS software requires deep integration with client billing logic and regulatory requirements, creating meaningful switching costs for established customers. Cerillion's telco-specific expertise is a barrier to entry against generic ERP or CRM vendors, while the scale required to build a credible global BSS platform protects against smaller challengers. The primary risk is customer concentration: a small number of telco clients can represent a large proportion of revenue, making individual contract wins and losses structurally significant.

## Key Risks
1. Customer concentration (HIGH): As a small-cap BSS vendor, Cerillion is likely to have meaningful revenue concentration among a small number of telecoms clients. The loss of a major telco account would have a disproportionate P&L impact. This is the single most important risk to monitor in any investor presentation.
2. Telco capex cyclicality (MEDIUM): Telecoms operators run multi-year network investment cycles. When capex is directed toward infrastructure (5G rollout, fibre build-out), BSS spending can be deferred. Cerillion's revenue is partially exposed to this cyclicality via new implementation revenue, even if recurring maintenance is more stable.
3. SaaS transition execution risk (MEDIUM): The migration from on-premise to cloud is the right strategic direction, but execution risk is real. Customer migration projects can slip, consume professional services resources, and temporarily suppress margins. The pace of transition directly affects the revenue quality investors are pricing the stock on.
4. Small-cap liquidity and register (MEDIUM): AIM-listed micro-caps with market caps below GBP200m typically lack sell-side coverage and have limited institutional register. This creates both a valuation discount and a liquidity risk for investors who need to size in or out of positions efficiently.
