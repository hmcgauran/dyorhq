## Executive Summary

## Business Model
Boeing designs, manufactures and services commercial jetliners (737, 747, 767, 777, 787 Dreamliner), military aircraft (F-15, F/A-18, KC-46 tanker, T-7 trainer, MV-22 Osprey) and space systems (Space Launch System, Starliner capsule, satellite systems). The commercial aeronautics business (BCA - Boeing Commercial Airplanes) accounts for approximately 35-40% of revenues and is the highest-margin segment when operating normally. Boeing Defense, Space & Security (BDS) provides defence platform stability. The core problem is BCA: Boeing has been unable to produce aircraft at the rate and quality standard required by airlines. The 737 MAX fleet is grounded or operating under scrutiny in multiple markets, and production rates have been capped by the FAA at 38 per month - well below the 52 per month originally planned. The 787 Dreamliner has also faced quality review. Boeing's labour force has been reduced and upskilled following the post-MAX hiring surge, but quality control systems remain under regulatory scrutiny. The commercial aircraft aftermarket (spare parts, MRO) provides revenue stability but cannot offset delivery shortfalls.

## Recent Catalysts
Boeing's operational crisis has continued: the January 2024 Alaska Airlines door plug blowout resulted in an FAA audit that capped 737 MAX production and placed Boeing under heightened regulatory oversight. CEO Dave Calhoun announced his resignation and a structural reorganisation is underway. The company raised $10 billion in equity in early 2024 to bolster its balance sheet - a significant dilutive event. The Starliner capsule finally achieved its first crewed test flight to the ISS in May 2024, a rare positive. The 737 MAX 7 and 737 MAX 10 certification delays have persisted, leaving the family incomplete. The Space Launch System continues to be Boeing's primary NASA human spaceflight vehicle, but delays and cost overruns are chronic.

## Key Risks
1. Production quality and safety recurrence: Any additional safety incident or quality failure (door plug repeat, engine failure, software fault) would likely trigger an immediate FAA production freeze, customer cancellations and potentially existential reputational damage.
2. FAA regulatory oversight: Boeing is under the most intensive regulatory scrutiny in its history. The FAA has stated it will not lift production caps until satisfied with Boeing's quality systems - a timeline entirely outside management control.
3. Customer and competitive position: Airbus is sold out through 2030+ and is the primary beneficiary of Boeing's crisis. Airlines unable to get Boeing deliveries are forced to the Airbus waiting list or secondary market. Boeing risks permanent market share loss.
4. Balance sheet and cash burn: Boeing has negative free cash flow and has already raised $10 billion in equity. Further capital raises would be dilutive. The defence business provides some cash flow buffer, but is not sufficient to sustain the commercial business alone.
5. Labour and manufacturing culture: Boeing's workforce has been restructured multiple times. The cultural and quality control issues identified by the FAA are systemic, not easily fixed by process documents. Restoring manufacturing excellence will take years.
