This structure provides revenue diversification across capability domains while allowing operational autonomy. MASS (Defence Electronics): The largest subsidiary by revenue. Specialist in electronic warfare (EW) systems, surveillance, and countermeasures. MASS supplies the UK MOD and export customers with EW suites, communication intelligence systems, and sensor packages. Long-standing supplier relationships and security-cleared workforce represent a meaningful barrier to entry. EID (Military Communications): Portuguese-headquartered military communications specialist. Designs and supplies tactical communications systems, including vehicle-mounted and soldier-worn equipment, to European and Middle Eastern defence ministries. Accretive geographic diversification to the group. SOE (Sonar and Acoustic Systems): Specialist in submarine and surface-ship sonar systems. Supplies the UK Royal Navy and international navies with acoustic sensors, torpedo detection systems, and underwater surveillance capabilities. The maritime domain is a stated UK and NATO investment priority. ASV (Autonomous Systems): Designs and supplies autonomous maritime vessels and unmanned surface vehicles (USVs). Growing exposure to the autonomous warfare theme, including participation in UK Navy autonomous trials. Smaller but fast-growing division. Revenue is predominantly contract-based with the UK MOD and export defence ministries. Contracts range from multi-year framework agreements to smaller technical services engagements. Export revenue provides geographic diversification and reduces UK budget concentration risk.

## Key Risks
1. UK MOD budget concentration (HIGH): A significant portion of group revenue is derived from UK MOD programmes. Any meaningful reduction in UK defence spending - whether from fiscal pressure, policy shift, or programme reprioritisation - would directly impact Cohort's top line. The ring-fenced defence budget commitment has political risk in a changing government environment.
2. Export approval dependency (MEDIUM): Export contracts to Middle Eastern and European customers require government-to-government approval. Political relationships and export licensing create uncertainty and delay risk that Cohort cannot control.
3. Contract timing volatility (MEDIUM): Large defence contracts are episodic. A gap between major programme completions and new awards can cause meaningful revenue lulls. This timing risk is structural to the business and not fully visible in reported order books.
4. Scale and liquidity (MEDIUM): At ~£280m market cap on AIM, CHRT lacks the analyst coverage, liquidity, and institutional investor base of larger defence names. The spread can be wide and large positions are difficult to build or exit without market impact.
