## Executive Summary
AIB Group plc is a-systemically important Irish retail and commercial bank, majority-owned by the Irish state following the 2010-era financial crisis recapitalisation. The bank occupies a dominant position in the Republic of Ireland's mortgage and SME lending markets and benefits from a structurally advantaged funding base through the Irish Post Office and AIB's own branch network. The Irish economy continues to outperform the eurozone average, supporting loan book quality, though the housing market remains a key sensitivity. The near-term case is complicated by the pace of ECB rate normalisation, which compresses net interest margin (NIM) faster than many models anticipate, and by the government's ongoing disposal programme, which introduces overhang risk into the register. At 846p, AIB trades at a meaningful discount to book value relative to higher-rated eurozone peers -- a discount that is partly rational given state ownership and partly an opportunity if the margin compression story is adequately priced in. Bottom line: HOLD at 846p. The margin outlook is softening but the loan book remains healthy and capital generation is strong. The government disposal programme is a medium-term overhang rather than an acute catalyst. For patient investors, AIB offers a reasonable yield with optionality on re-rating if the ROTE trajectory is sustained and disposal progress accelerates.

## Business Model
AIB operates three primary segments: Republic of Ireland Retail Banking, Republic of Ireland Commercial Banking, and Foreign Chambers (principally Northern Ireland and UK operations via First Trust Bank). The bank's revenue is derived from four principal streams: Net interest income (NII) is the dominant earnings driver, accounting for approximately 70% of total income. AIB earns the spread between the rate it pays on deposits and the rate it charges on loans -- primarily mortgages, SME term loans, and corporate facilities. The Irish deposit base is historically sticky and low-cost relative to wholesale-funded peers. Following the ECB tightening cycle, AIB benefited from a significant NIM expansion as loan portfolios repriced faster than deposit pricing. Non-interest income comprises transaction fees, foreign exchange revenue, insurance commissions, and card payment income. This segment is modest but recurring and provides some diversification from the pure-rate-sensitive NII stream. Loan book composition: Irish residential mortgages represent approximately 40% of the loan book. SME and corporate lending accounts for a further 35%. The remainder is commercial real estate (CRE) and other specialist lending. The mortgage book is predominantly tracker and standard variable rate (SVR) -- a function of Ireland's historical product mix -- which means the book is rate-sensitive but also benefits from upward rate cycles. The competitive position is strong in retail and SME. In corporate banking, AIB competes with Bank of Ireland, Ulster Bank (in wind-down), and a small number of international banks. The Irish market is concentrated, which limits competitive intensity and supports pricing discipline.

The market has broadly accepted the margin compression thesis, but further ECB cuts beyond current pricing would pressure 2026 earnings. Irish housing market dynamics: Ireland's housing shortage remains acute. Housing completions are running below demand, keeping property values relatively firm. Mortgage drawdowns remained robust through 2024-2025, supporting the loan book. However, affordability constraints -- driven by income, employment, and existing mortgage rates -- are moderating demand. Drawdown volumes are expected to decline modestly in 2026. Government disposal programme: The Irish Government (NTMA) has been a consistent seller of AIB shares since 2017. The stake has reduced from ~71% to approximately 54% as of early 2026. Disposal pace is discretionary and market-sensitive. Any acceleration would represent an overhang. The government has signalled a preference for complete exit but has not committed to a timeline. Capital return and dividends: AIB has been consistently returning capital via dividends and share buybacks. The CET1 ratio of ~17.5% is well above regulatory requirements and supports continued capital return capacity through the cycle. Cost-income ratio pressure: AIB's cost base has been subject to investment in digital transformation, which is necessary but creates near-term efficiency headwinds. The cost-income ratio expanded to approximately 56% in FY2025 from 52% in FY2023, reflecting both the investment spend and the NIM compression environment.

## Key Risks
1. Irish housing market correction (HIGH): AIB's mortgage book and CRE exposure are directly tied to Irish property values. A 15-20% house price correction -- plausible in a severe stress scenario given affordability stretched at current rates -- would increase LTV ratios on the mortgage book, accelerate Stage 2 migrations, and require material additional provisions. This is the primary tail risk.
2. NIM compression from ECB rate cuts (HIGH): AIB's tracker mortgage book reprices immediately with ECB rate cuts. Deposit competition from credit unions, Post Office accounts, and fintech challengers limits the ability to offset by cutting liability costs. If the ECB cuts faster than the market prices, FY2026 and FY2027 earnings will be lower than current consensus.
3. Government disposal overhang (MEDIUM): The NTMA holds ~54% and has been a consistent seller. Any large block transaction or accelerated programme could create sustained price pressure, particularly if market liquidity is thin. The disposal overhang is a structural feature of the register and limits re-rating potential until the stake is materially lower.
4. Credit quality normalisation (MEDIUM): Post-COVID provision releases have been a tailwind for earnings in recent years. As the stock of distressed loans normalises, the headwind from new provision formation versus release income will reverse. The Stage 2 loan ratio of ~8.3% warrants monitoring -- a shift to 10%+ would signal incipient credit stress.
5. Competitive intensity in SME lending (MEDIUM): Bank of Ireland remains a direct competitor in all core segments. Ulster Bank's wind-down created temporary opportunity but the market is re-equilibrating. New entrants (fintech lenders, debt funds) are targeting specific niches but are not systemic competitors yet.
