## Business Model
Visa operates the world's largest electronic payments network, connecting consumers, merchants, financial institutions and governments across more than 200 countries. The company does not take credit risk on transactions; it earns fees from financial institution clients (issuers) for processing transactions, as well as from merchants for certain payment processing services. Key revenue streams include: service revenues (fees paid by issuers based on payment volume), data processing revenues (transaction processing fees), international transaction revenues (cross-border fees) and other revenues (value-added services, Visa Consulting Analytics). The company has a highly capital-light model with high margins and exceptional free cash flow conversion.

## Key Risks
1. Regulatory risk on interchange: Interchange fees (the fees merchants pay to card networks and issuers) are under regulatory scrutiny globally. The EU's MIF regulations, Australia's caps and potential US regulatory action represent a structural risk to Visa's revenue model.
2. Real-time payment competition: The US Federal Reserve's FedNow instant payment rail, The Clearing House's RTP network and emerging real-time payment infrastructures globally could over time disintermediate card networks for certain payment types.
3. Cybersecurity and fraud: A major data breach or systemic fraud event affecting the Visa network would be highly damaging to brand trust and could trigger regulatory penalties and card replacement costs.
4. Valuation: At 28.57x P/E, Visa is not cheap. The market is pricing in continued execution on the global payments secular growth thesis; any misstep would cause multiple compression.
