## Business Model
HCA operates approximately 180 acute care hospitals and over 2,000 sites of care across 20 states and the United Kingdom, making it the largest hospital operator in the US by revenue and bed count. The business model is grounded in the provision of inpatient hospital services (surgical, medical, emergency/trauma) supplemented by outpatient services, diagnostic centres and a growing physician services platform. Revenue is primarily fee-for-service, reimbursed by Medicare, Medicaid and commercial insurers. HCA's competitive advantages include its scale (negotiating leverage with payers), its concentrated geographic footprint in high-growth Sunbelt markets, its proprietary data on clinical outcomes, and its ability to recruit physicians in markets where it is often the dominant or sole hospital employer. Capital allocation has been disciplined, with regular share buybacks and a growing dividend.

## Key Risks
1. Healthcare labour cost inflation: Registered nurses, physicians and allied health professionals remain in tight supply. Agency and traveller nurse costs have moderated from post-pandemic peaks but remain elevated and represent a structural margin risk if hospital occupancy normalises.
2. Medicare reimbursement pressure: As a large recipient of Medicare revenue, any legislative or regulatory action to reduce Medicare reimbursement rates would directly impair HCA's revenue and margins. The political environment for hospital pricing reform is a persistent concern.
3. Commercial payer leverage: Large employer consortia and insurers are increasingly scrutinising hospital pricing. If HCA is seen as a high-cost provider in a given market, it risks losing favourable network status, which would directly impact volumes.
4. Outpatient shift and site-of-care competition: The broader shift to outpatient and ambulatory settings is a strategic opportunity but also exposes HCA to competition from independent ASC operators and physician-owned facilities that do not carry the same cost structure.
