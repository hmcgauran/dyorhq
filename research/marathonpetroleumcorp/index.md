## Business Model
Marathon Petroleum is the largest US refiner by crude processing capacity, operating 13 refineries with a combined capacity of approximately 3.3 million barrels per day. The asset base is heavily concentrated in the US Midwest and Gulf Coast - the mostsignificant crude oil production regions in North America - giving Marathon a significant feedstock cost advantage relative to coastal competitors. The core model is crude oil refining into petrol, diesel, jet fuel, and asphalt. The midcontinent refining system is advantaged by its proximity to Bakken and Permian Basin crude production, enabling the company to purchase discounted domestic crude and sell refined products at regional crack spreads that can be structurally superior to coastal spreads when logistics are tight. The Midstream segment operatespipelines, terminals, and gathering systems through a combination of wholly-owned assets and the Marathon Petroleum Corp LP (MPC LP) master limited partnership. The Speedway retail network (approximately 3,200 convenience stores) was sold to 7-Eleven in 2022 for $22 billion - this disposal significantly strengthened the balance sheet and provided capital for buybacks and debt reduction. Capital allocation has been shareholder-friendly: following the Speedway sale, MPC has been aggressively buying back shares and maintaining a base dividend with supplementary quarterly dividends tied to performance. At current crude prices, the business generates very substantial free cash flow.

## Key Risks
1. Refining margin cyclicality: The most important near-term risk. US refinery crack spreads are highly cyclical and sensitive to global supply-demand balances, imports, and inventory cycles. The extraordinary margins of 2022-2023 have normalised and any return to those levels requires genuine supply disruptions or demand surprises.
2. Medium-term petrol demand destruction from EV penetration: Electric vehicles represent a structural headwind for petrol demand. Marathon's Midwest assets face lower immediate EV penetration risk than coastal California assets, but over a 5-10 year horizon this is a real headwind. Marathon's large asset base is less flexible than some competitors in switching to renewable diesel or sustainable aviation fuel.
3. Crude oil price volatility: Marathon's assets are configured for specific crude slates (Bakken, Permian, Canadian heavy). A sudden shift in crude price dynamics or logistics can change feedstock economics rapidly.
4. Larger asset base limits optionality: With 3.3 million bpd of refining capacity, Marathon has limited ability to meaningfully curtail operations in a downturn without taking significant writedowns. This makes the company more exposed to trough-margin environments than smaller, more flexible regional refiners.
5. Geopolitical tariff risk: Any significant disruption to North American trade flows from tariff escalation could create crack spread volatility - particularly in the Midwest where pipeline connectivity to the USGC can constrain the ability to redirect product flows.
