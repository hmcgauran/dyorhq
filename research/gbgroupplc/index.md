## Business Model
GB Group operates a data-as-a-service identity verification platform across four primary product lines: identity verification (ID verification, document checks, biometric matching), address verification (postcode and utility data matching), fraud detection (Velocity checks, phone intelligence, device fingerprinting), and KYC/compliance tools (screening, watchlist matching, ongoing monitoring). The platform processes hundreds of millions of identity checks annually across a blue-chip customer base of banks, building societies, fintechs, telecommunications providers, and e-commerce platforms. Revenue model: GBG's revenue is predominantly recurring. Enterprise contracts typically run 2-3 years with minimum commitment levels. Transaction-based pricing adds a variable component that scales with customer volumes -- a beneficial dynamic as digital onboarding rates increase. The mix between subscription and transaction revenue varies by segment, with the Identity segment more subscription-weighted and Fraud Detection more transaction-driven. Geographic footprint: The UK is the largest market, generating approximately 55% of group revenue. The US is the primary growth market, now contributing roughly 20% of revenue. Australia, Europe (principally Germany, Netherlands, Spain), and Asia-Pacific contribute the remainder. The US expansion has been a strategic focus, though it carries higher customer acquisition costs and longer enterprise sales cycles than the domestic UK market. Competitive position: GBG occupies a mid-market position between pure-play regional identity verification providers (e.g.,gb Group's legacy GB Ech contactless ID check tools) and global platform giants (Onfido, Jumio, LexisNexis). Its moat derives from proprietary data assets -- particularly the UK Address Management File and associated PAF-derived data -- and from deep regulatory compliance integration with UK and European AML frameworks. The address data asset is particularly durable: it is refreshed continuously, costly to replicate, and directly required by FCA and EU AML regulation.

## Key Risks
1. US go-to-market execution risk (HIGH): GBG's valuation rerating depends substantially on US revenue growth. The US enterprise identity verification market is more competitive and has longer sales cycles than the UK. If the US pipeline conversion rate disappoints for a second consecutive year, the multiple compression will be significant and durable. This is the single most important risk factor for the investment case.
2. Fraud segment competitive pressure (MEDIUM-HIGH): The Velocity checking and fraud detection market is crowded. LexisNexis (RELX), Acxiom (IPG), and Thales have significant proprietary data assets that are difficult for GBG to replicate. Price competition in the fraud segment has increased, particularly in the US, and if GBG fails to differentiate on data quality versus price, the fraud segment's contribution to group EBITDA will be constrained.
3. Integration and platform migration execution (MEDIUM): GBG's transition from legacy infrastructure to a unified platform is ongoing. Execution failures, customer disruption during migration, or data quality issues in the consolidated platform could trigger customer churn in the fraud segment. This risk is partially mitigated by the subscription revenue model but remains material for the US expansion narrative.
4. Acquisition debt and balance sheet leverage (MEDIUM): The debt-funded acquisition created a net debt position of approximately 1.5x EBITDA. If EBITDA growth stalls or disappoints, leverage metrics deteriorate and restrict operational flexibility. The refinancing reduces this risk materially, but it does not eliminate it entirely. Net debt/EBITDA above 2x would be a warning signal.
5. UK macro and financial services spending risk (MEDIUM): A meaningful deterioration in UK financial services activity -- particularly in the banking and fintech sub-sectors where GBG has its highest customer concentration -- could reduce transaction volumes and delay enterprise contract renewals. The current UK macro environment is cautious, and discretionary spending on regtech is at risk in a budget-constrained environment.
6. Regulatory and data privacy risk (MEDIUM): Identity data processing is subject to GDPR, UK GDPR, and an evolving US state-level privacy landscape. Regulatory changes that restrict the use of certain data assets for identity verification -- particularly around biometric data or credit-header data -- could require product changes and impose compliance costs. The UK's new DCT (Digital ID and Attributes Trust) framework is broadly constructive but implementation uncertainty remains.
