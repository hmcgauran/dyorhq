## Business Model
Boku operates a two-sided payments network connecting digital merchants to mobile network operators. The company provides three core services: (1) carrier billing -- merchants add Boku's payment APIs and consumers can charge purchases to their mobile phone bill, particularly valuable in markets where card penetration is low; (2) mobile wallet top-up -- direct top-up of mobile money accounts via the MNO relationship; and (3) identity verification and fraud prevention -- using mobile operator data to authenticate users and detect fraudulent transactions without requiring personal data. The revenue model is transaction-based, with Boku earning a share of each payment processed. Because the infrastructure is largely built and the marginal cost of new merchants is low, incremental revenue flows at high margins. The MNO relationships represent the core moat -- these contracts take years to negotiate and Boku has accumulated relationships with operators across more than 60 countries. Switching costs for merchants are meaningful since integration requires technical work, and removing Boku would require renegotiating directly with dozens of MNOs. Key merchant verticals: subscription streaming, gaming, digital goods, and utilities. Top clients include Netflix, Spotify, Disney+, and several major Asian digital platforms.

## Key Risks

2. #2 Competitive pressure from digital wallets: Apple Pay, Google Pay, Alipay, and regional e-wallets are expanding globally. If these platforms reduce the need for carrier billing, Boku's addressable market shrinks.
3. #3 AIM liquidity and re-rating risk: The shares trade on AIM, limiting institutional ownership. Until there is a catalyst for a standard listing or index inclusion, the stock may remain range-bound despite solid operating performance.
4. #4 Emerging market FX risk: A significant portion of revenue is generated in Asia and Latin America. Local currency depreciation against sterling would suppress reported revenue and earnings.
