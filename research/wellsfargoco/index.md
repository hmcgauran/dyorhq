## Executive Summary

## Business Model
Wells Fargo is one of the 'Big Four' US banks, with approximately $1.9 trillion in assets. The company operates four operating segments: Consumer Banking (checking, savings, credit cards, auto loans), Commercial Banking (corporate lending, treasury management, real estate banking), Investment and Wealth Management (wealth management, brokerage, private banking) and the Fixed Income, Currencies and Commodities (FICC) trading desk. Wells Fargo has a large and sticky deposit base from its consumer banking franchise (approximately 70 million customers). The company is subject to an asset cap (cannot exceed $1.95 trillion in total assets) imposed by the Federal Reserve following the 2016 fake accounts scandal, which has limited its ability to grow its balance sheet.

The net interest margin has been pressured by the inverted yield curve, but a normalisation (shorter-term rates falling relative to longer-term rates) would be a significant tailwind for NII. Credit quality has remained resilient, with charge-off rates lower than feared. The company has been repurchasing shares opportunistically as the stock has traded near the asset cap.

## Key Risks
1. Asset cap uncertainty: The Fed's asset cap on Wells Fargo has been in place since 2018 and has materially constrained the company's growth. Its removal timeline is uncertain, and continued restrictions would cap the earnings recovery.
2. Credit cycle risk: Wells Fargo's large commercial real estate (CRE) exposure (particularly office and multi-family) is a concern in a higher-for-longer rate environment. Office vacancy rates in major cities remain elevated.
3. Yield curve sensitivity: As a bank with a large deposit base and significant loan portfolio, Wells Fargo is highly sensitive to the shape of the yield curve. Prolonged inversion compresses NIM and earnings.
4. Historical risk: The 2016 fake accounts scandal, the Enron-era legacy and other compliance failures have resulted in the most restrictive consent order environment for any major US bank, constraining operational flexibility.
