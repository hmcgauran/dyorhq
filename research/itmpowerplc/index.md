## Business Model
ITM Power designs, manufactures, and sells large-scale PEM (proton exchange membrane) electrolyser systems. Its core products are megawatt-scale hydrogen production units targeted at industrial customers, energy storage operators, and, critically, the emerging ecosystem of green hydrogen producers feeding into hard-to-abate sectors: steel, ammonia, refining, and transport. Revenue is project-based and lumpy -- driven by bespoke system sales and long-cycle procurement processes. The company has historically been reliant on a small number of large contracts. This creates concentration risk and makes revenue timing difficult to predict. The shift towards standardised, repeatable product configurations is ongoing but incomplete. Strategic partnerships are the clearest near-term commercial asset. The Siemens relationship provides technology co-development and a route to large industrial energy users. The Shell relationship is a reference customer and channel partner. These partnerships are necessary but not sufficient -- both Shell and Siemens have made diversifying hydrogen supplier relationships explicit. The PEM technology advantage is real but not exclusive. Competing electrolyser architectures -- particularly alkaline and solid oxide -- claim lower capex at scale. ITM's differentiation rests on faster response times, higher current density, and the ability to pair with variable renewable power sources -- an important advantage as grid-connected hydrogen production grows.

ITM is well-positioned in the UK, but deployment volumes remain far below the government's own targets, limiting near-term revenue visibility. European green hydrogen investment: The EU's Hydrogen Bank and the Important Projects of Common European Interest (IPCEI) framework have committed significant public capital to green hydrogen. ITM's European footprint -- through the Siemens relationship in particular -- provides access to these funding streams, but competition from larger European manufacturers is intense. Shell and Siemens partnership development: Ongoing, but neither partner has given ITM exclusive status. Both energy majors are simultaneously backing competing electrolyser manufacturers and developing in-house capability. This is a necessary relationship but not a protective moat. Steel and industrial decarbonisation deals: Several European steel producers have announced hydrogen reduction pathways that include PEM electrolyser procurement. ITM's 1MW-plus product range is relevant here, but the conversion pipeline from MOU to firm order is long and variable. Capital raise risk: As a loss-making company with capital-intensive operations, ITM will likely need to return to equity markets. This is a structural overhang for existing shareholders and a material risk to conviction score if it occurs at a depressed share price.

## Key Risks
1. Hydrogen adoption timing (HIGH): The green hydrogen cost curve has not bent as quickly as optimists projected. Green hydrogen is still significantly more expensive than grey (steam methane reforming) or blue (CCS-enabled) hydrogen in most markets. Until the cost gap closes, demand will be subsidy-dependent and therefore policy-exposed.
2. Capital raise risk (HIGH): Loss-making, capital-intensive businesses that depend on project-based revenue are inherently vulnerable to dilution events. A capital raise at 70-80 GBX would be significantly dilutive to existing shareholders. This is not a question of if, but when and at what price.
3. Partner dependency (MEDIUM): Shell and Siemens are both strategic partners and customers. Both are actively diversifying their hydrogen supply chains. The loss of either partner as a reference customer would be a significant negative catalyst and a signal that ITM's technology is not winning in competitive tenders.
4. Competition from larger players (MEDIUM): Nel Hydrogen, Plug Power, and Cummins are all larger, better-capitalised electrolyser manufacturers. ITM's small-cap status is a disadvantage in large industrial procurement processes, where customers often require scale guarantees that ITM cannot confidently provide.
5. AIM liquidity and investor base (MEDIUM): ITM trades on LSE AIM, which carries inherently lower liquidity and a more limited institutional investor base than the main market. This increases price volatility and means the stock can trade at a persistent discount to fundamentally comparable companies on larger exchanges.
